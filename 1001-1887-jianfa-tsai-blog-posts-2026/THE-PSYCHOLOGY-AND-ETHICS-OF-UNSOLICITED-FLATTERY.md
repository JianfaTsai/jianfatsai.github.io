### ELI5 Summary

If someone who usually doesn't help you suddenly starts acting super nice for no clear reason, they are probably trying to trick you or want something from you that they aren't mentioning.

### Etymological and Cultural Context

The Chinese proverb "**无事献殷勤，非奸即盗**" (Wú shì xiàn yīn qín, fēi jiān jí dào) literally translates to: "One who is being unusually attentive without a reason is either a lecher or a thief." Historically, this phrase emerged from a societal structure where social interactions were governed by strict hierarchies and reciprocal obligations (Confucian ethics). In such a world, spontaneous displays of affection or service from a social inferior or a stranger were viewed with extreme skepticism, as they disrupted the established "social equilibrium" (Hwang, 1987).

### Philosophical and Psychological Analysis

The proverb functions as a heuristic—a mental shortcut—to protect the individual from **Social Engineering** and **Reciprocity Manipulation**.

- **The Reciprocity Principle:** According to social psychology, humans have an innate drive to return favors. When someone "offers kindness without cause," they are often pre-loading a debt. By accepting the small kindness, the victim feels psychologically obligated to comply with a much larger, future request (Cialdini, 2021).
    
- **The "Dark Triad" Correlation:** The "奸" (jiān/treacherous) and "盗" (dào/thief) components of the proverb align with modern descriptions of Machiavellianism. These individuals use strategic friendliness as a tool for "impression management" to mask their true ulterior motives (Jones & Paulhus, 2014).

### Nuances and Edge Cases

While the proverb is a powerful defensive tool, applying it too rigidly can lead to **Cynical Realism**, where one becomes unable to recognize genuine altruism.

1. **Altruism:** Some people perform acts of kindness simply because it aligns with their internal values, not because they want an external reward (Batson, 2011).
    
2. **Cultural Differences:** In high-context cultures (like China), silence and "face-saving" are paramount, making the "unspoken" motive more common. In low-context cultures, directness is preferred, so "unsolicited zeal" is often seen as a sales tactic rather than a moral failing.
    

### Counter-Argument

A potential counter-argument is that this mindset fosters a **Low-Trust Society**. If every act of kindness is met with suspicion ("He must want something"), it stifles the formation of new social bonds and discourages prosocial behavior. This can lead to a "Prisoner's Dilemma" scenario where no one cooperates because they fear being exploited, ultimately making the community less efficient and more isolated.

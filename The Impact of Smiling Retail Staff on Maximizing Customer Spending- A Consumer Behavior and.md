### Supportive Reasoning
Authentic smiles trigger emotional contagion, where customers unconsciously mirror expressions and experience heightened pleasure, leading to longer dwell times and higher basket values (Otterbring, 2017). Field experiments report 9% average spending increases with greeters and 8–15% basket value growth when staff smile and acknowledge customers (as synthesized in retail economics reviews, 2025). Retailers like Target have implemented “10-4” policies requiring smiles within set distances, citing improved holiday sales (Target corporate communications, 2025). From a retailer perspective, this low-cost intervention scales easily, fostering loyalty and immediate purchases via feelings-as-information theory, where positive mood signals “this is a good place to buy more.”

### Counter-Arguments
Forced or inauthentic smiles backfire, evoking distrust and reducing satisfaction, especially when customers perceive choice restrictions or commercial intent (Hofmann et al., 2024; Grandey et al., 2019). In luxury contexts, broad smiles lower engagement and purchase intent by signaling lower status or excessive eagerness (2025 advertising studies). Employee well-being suffers from emotional labor demands, risking burnout and higher turnover (Hochschild, 1983, updated in modern retail analyses). Culturally, smiles may not universally signal warmth—some demographics interpret them as insincere or intrusive—while Australian shoppers, valuing authenticity, may react negatively to overly scripted friendliness, potentially decreasing spending.

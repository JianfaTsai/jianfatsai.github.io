### Findings
Scholarly analysis reveals that Anubis, known anciently as Inpu or Anpu, functioned as a multifaceted deity central to ancient Egyptian funerary practices from the Predynastic Period through the Ptolemaic and Roman eras (Mark, 2016; Gray & Dunn, 2024). Researchers identify his primary roles as overseer of mummification, protector of the deceased, and guide of souls during the Weighing of the Heart ceremony, where he balanced the heart against the feather of Ma'at to determine worthiness for the afterlife (Willems, 1998; Bontempo, 2015). Historians note his iconography—a black jackal or jackal-headed man—symbolized regeneration, Nile fertility, and vigilance against grave desecration, with black evoking both decay and rebirth rather than evil (Ikram, 2015, as cited in Mark, 2016). In Late Antiquity, syncretism with Hermes produced Hermanubis, expanding his intermediary role between living, dead, and divine realms in magical and ritual contexts (Feliciano, n.d.). Peer-reviewed sources emphasize temporal evolution: Anubis dominated early funerary cults before Osiris's ascendancy in the Middle Kingdom, reflecting shifting theological priorities amid political centralization (Quertinmont, 2020; Magdolen, 2021). No disinformation appears in core Egyptological literature; however, popular media occasionally misportrays him as a malevolent figure, ignoring his protective ethos. Cross-domain insights link his symbolism to modern existential psychology and ethical leadership frameworks, offering scalable lessons for personal resilience without invoking pseudohistorical claims.

### Detailed Analysis
Ancient Egyptian theology positioned Anubis as a liminal figure bridging life and death, with duties encompassing embalming invention, tomb guardianship, and soul adjudication, underscoring a cultural valuation of bodily integrity for eternal existence (Patterson, n.d.; Gray & Dunn, 2024). Supportive reasoning highlights his embodiment of fidelity: as "He Who Is in the Place of Embalming," he modeled meticulous care, teaching that thorough preparation mitigates uncertainty in transitions (Willems, 1998). Historiographically, early depictions from the First Dynasty (c. 3150–2890 BCE) reflect Predynastic concerns with jackal scavenging, evolving into sophisticated justice motifs by the New Kingdom, demonstrating adaptive theological innovation amid empire expansion (Bontempo, 2015). Cross-domain integration draws parallels to contemporary thanatology, where awareness of mortality fosters meaning-making, and to Aristotelian ethics, wherein habitual virtue (as in heart-weighing) determines eudaimonia (flourishing). 

Counter-arguments note potential overemphasis on death preparation risked neglecting earthly vitality, as critiqued in some Egyptological reviews of Old Kingdom tomb focus (David, 2003, as cited in Mark, 2016). Devil’s advocate evaluation reveals source biases: Greco-Roman authors like Plutarch introduced syncretic lenses potentially Hellenizing Anubis's chthonic aspects for cultural assimilation, while 19th–20th century Egyptology occasionally exoticized deities through colonial frameworks before post-1960s archaeological rigor corrected temporal contexts (Feliciano, n.d.; Quertinmont, 2020). Nuances include regional variations—Upper Egypt's Cynopolis cult versus nationwide amuletic use—and edge cases, such as his rare punitive role against tomb robbers, balancing benevolence with accountability (Pinch, 2004). Implications extend to organizational use: leaders might adopt Anubis-like guardianship for knowledge preservation in crises. Real-world scalability appears in mindfulness practices promoting legacy reflection, though modern secular contexts demand caution against literal supernaturalism. Multiple perspectives affirm Anubis's humanistic core—protection and truth—while acknowledging polytheistic fluidity absent in monotheistic traditions. No misinformation undermines these peer-reviewed interpretations, as genetic studies confirm the "jackal" as an African wolf hybrid, refining rather than refuting symbolism (Ikram, 2015).

### Abstract
This case study examines the problem of unintentional duplicate book acquisitions arising from memory lapses in personal collections. Drawing on consumer behavior research and the user's reported experiences, the analysis evaluates the proposed solution of exclusive Amazon purchasing while highlighting more comprehensive alternatives such as dedicated book cataloging applications. Findings indicate that while digital platforms offer partial purchase reminders, personal inventory tools provide broader protection across all retail channels. The discussion balances benefits and limitations, incorporates Australian consumer law perspectives, and offers actionable recommendations grounded in peer-reviewed insights on cognitive and habitual purchasing patterns.

### Keywords
duplicate purchases, consumer memory, book cataloging, personal inventory management, Australian Consumer Law

### Glossary
- **Duplicate purchases**: Acquiring the same item more than once due to oversight rather than deliberate choice.
- **Consumer memory**: The cognitive process by which individuals recall prior acquisitions, often impaired by habit or information overload.
- **Personal inventory management**: Systematic tracking of owned goods using manual lists or digital applications to prevent redundancy.
- **Australian Consumer Law (ACL)**: Federal legislation governing fair trading and consumer protections, administered by the Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC).

### Introduction
Duplicate book purchases represent a common yet understudied facet of consumer behavior in which individuals unintentionally acquire multiple copies of the same title. This pattern stems from ordinary memory limitations rather than deliberate excess and incurs avoidable financial and spatial costs (Nori et al., 2022). In the present case, the independent researcher Jianfa Tsai describes repeated instances of forgetting physical bookstore acquisitions and subsequently repurchasing identical titles online (Tsai, personal communication, April 20, 2026). The proposed remedy—restricting all purchases to Amazon for its purchase-history notifications—warrants critical examination. This article employs a case-study approach informed by peer-reviewed literature on compulsive and habitual buying to assess the solution's viability and to propose refined strategies.

### Federal, State, or Local Laws in Australia
Under the Australian Consumer Law (ACL), which applies uniformly to online and physical retail transactions, consumers enjoy identical guarantees regardless of purchase venue (Australian Competition and Consumer Commission [ACCC], 2025a). The ACL mandates that goods be of acceptable quality and fit for purpose, yet it does not address forgetfulness-induced duplicates; such errors fall under buyer responsibility rather than retailer liability (ACCC, 2025b). State and territory fair-trading bodies, such as Consumer Affairs Victoria, echo this framework and provide guidance on refunds only when products prove faulty or misdescribed. No federal, state, or local statute requires retailers to maintain or disclose individual purchase histories for the purpose of preventing redundancy. Privacy legislation under the Privacy Act 1988 further restricts how platforms like Amazon may use purchase data, balancing consumer convenience against data-protection obligations. Thus, while the ACL ensures baseline protections, it offers no direct remedy for memory-related duplicate spending (ACCC, 2025a).

### Methods
The present analysis adopts a mixed-methods case-study design consistent with qualitative consumer-behavior research. First, the user's reported experiences served as the primary case narrative. Second, a targeted review of peer-reviewed sources on repeat purchasing, cognitive influences on buying, and habit formation supplemented the case. ### Results
User accounts reveal that bookstore acquisitions frequently escape recall, prompting secondary online purchases and resulting in redundant holdings. Peer-reviewed findings corroborate this pattern: cognitive factors such as working-memory limitations contribute to compulsive or habitual buying, while reminder impulses triggered by advertisements exacerbate repetition (Nori et al., 2022; Hidayat, 2022). Amazon's order-history function provides visible records of past transactions, yet forum users report inconsistent on-product warnings for physical books, particularly when items originate from third-party sellers (Amazon Forum, 2021). Dedicated cataloging applications, by contrast, enable universal scanning of ISBN barcodes across all acquisition channels, thereby closing coverage gaps.

### Supportive Reasoning
Proponents of platform-specific tracking correctly note that centralized purchase histories reduce some redundancy risks within a single ecosystem. Research on habitual consumer behavior demonstrates that external cues, such as digital reminders, can interrupt automatic repurchase cycles (Tohidi, 2022). When applied diligently, such systems align with best practices in personal finance by minimizing waste. Furthermore, integrating catalog apps extends this benefit universally, empowering users to verify ownership before any transaction irrespective of vendor.

### Counter-Arguments
Critics observe that exclusive reliance on any single retailer fails to encompass offline or multi-platform acquisitions, leaving significant exposure unaddressed (Tsai, personal communication, April 20, 2026). Empirical studies highlight that cognitive biases persist regardless of platform cues; working-memory deficits can override even accurate reminders (Nori et al., 2022). Additionally, over-dependence on corporate data systems raises privacy concerns under Australian law, and platform policy changes—such as altered warning displays—can erode reliability without notice (Amazon Forum, 2022). Finally, the financial and environmental costs of duplicate accumulation extend beyond immediate dollars to long-term clutter and resource inefficiency.

### Discussion
The tension between partial digital tracking and comprehensive personal inventory underscores a broader truth in consumer psychology: external tools succeed only when paired with deliberate user habits. Historiographical scrutiny of the cited studies reveals that early 21st-century research emphasized impulse control, whereas recent work foregrounds environmental cues and technology mediation, reflecting evolving retail landscapes (Mohammadi, 2021). Edge cases, such as gifted books or secondhand acquisitions, further complicate reliance on any single solution. Cross-domain insights from cognitive science and personal-finance literature converge on the superiority of user-controlled inventories over vendor-dependent systems.

### Real-Life Examples
Book collectors frequently report acquiring duplicate editions after misplacing receipts or neglecting manual logs. Similarly, hobbyists in other domains—such as tool enthusiasts or vinyl-record collectors—describe parallel experiences resolved through barcode-scanning applications. These anecdotes mirror the user's Melbourne-based scenario and illustrate the scalability of inventory practices across diverse consumer profiles.

### Wise Perspectives
Consumer-behavior experts advocate proactive memory aids over reactive vendor notifications, emphasizing that "habits form in stable contexts, but disruptions such as multi-channel shopping demand active intervention" (Tohidi, 2022, p. 12). Independent researchers like Tsai similarly counsel integrating psychological awareness with practical tools to break emotional-spending cycles (Tsai, 2026).

### Conclusion
Duplicate book purchases arise from ordinary cognitive limitations yet yield measurable financial and spatial consequences. While Amazon's tracking offers limited utility, robust personal cataloging applications afford superior, vendor-agnostic protection. By cataloging existing collections and verifying ownership prior to each purchase, consumers can curtail redundancy effectively.

### Risks
Potential drawbacks include initial time investment in cataloging, application subscription costs (though many free tiers exist), and the risk of over-reliance on technology that may itself fail or become obsolete. Data-privacy exposure remains a secondary concern when using cloud-based inventories.

### Immediate Consequences
Duplicate acquisitions result in immediate monetary loss and physical clutter, diverting resources from other priorities and potentially straining household budgets.

### Long-Term Consequences
Over time, unchecked duplication fosters collection bloat, storage challenges, and opportunity costs. Psychologically, repeated forgetfulness may erode confidence in personal financial management and contribute to broader patterns of wasteful consumption.

### Improvements
Enhance the proposed Amazon-only approach by combining it with a dedicated inventory application. Users should scan all existing books upon implementation and establish a pre-purchase verification routine. Regular audits of the digital catalog further strengthen accuracy.

### Authorities & Organizations To Seek Help From
The Australian Competition and Consumer Commission (ACCC) offers general guidance on consumer rights, while state bodies such as Consumer Affairs Victoria provide localized advice. For psychological aspects of habitual buying, community mental-health services or financial-counseling organizations such as the National Debt Helpline can assist. Library associations and independent-research networks may offer cataloging workshops.

### Action Steps
1. Inventory current books by scanning ISBN barcodes with a free or low-cost application such as Libib or LibraryThing.
2. Before any new purchase—whether in-store or online—consult the personal catalog.
3. Maintain purchase receipts digitally within the application for cross-verification.
4. Schedule quarterly reviews to prune duplicates or donate surplus copies.
5. Educate household members on the shared inventory system to prevent collective overspending.

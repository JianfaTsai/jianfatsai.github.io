### Evidence
Peer-reviewed research demonstrates that consumers increasingly favor at-home coffee preparation amid rising prices and habit formation, with segmentation studies revealing distinct groups motivated by cost, convenience, and quality perceptions (Czarniecka-Skubina et al., 2021, https://doi.org/10.3390/ijerph18083948). Longitudinal data on repurchase behavior at coffee chains indicate that habitual out-of-home purchases are driven more by environmental cues than deliberate choice, supporting preemptive home consumption as a disruptor of automatic spending (Kim et al., 2019, as cited in behavioral economics analyses). Additionally, caffeine's arousing effects prior to shopping have been shown to elevate impulsivity and expenditure, underscoring the value of home-based routines to mitigate such influences (as evidenced in field experiments on consumer spending; see related findings in peer-reviewed consumer psychology literature) (Mahoney et al., 2019, https://doi.org/10.1016/j.clnu.2018.04.004).

Cross-domain insights from psychology highlight habit disruption as key to sustained change, yet nuances arise in edge cases such as time-poor professionals or those deriving social utility from cafe visits (Samoggia & Riedel, 2019). ### Counter-Arguments

In high-pressure lifestyles, the convenience of out-of-home options may outweigh marginal savings, and overemphasis on individual habits risks ignoring structural economic factors like wage stagnation (Baylan, 2017). Caffeine timing at home could inadvertently heighten arousal before outings, paradoxically influencing other expenditures (related consumer psychology findings).

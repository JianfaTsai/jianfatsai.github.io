### Problem Statement
In an era of rapid AI proliferation and surging university graduate numbers, traditional education pathways risk commoditizing logical-analytical skills while undervaluing irreplaceable human traits like creativity and ethical reasoning (Portocarrero Ramos, 2025). Parents face the challenge of preparing children for uncertain futures: Is it more effective to prioritize imaginative and moral foundations before age 8, as the user suggests, or does this overlook lifelong brain adaptability and potential risks of delayed academic rigor? The user's brain-side dichotomy introduces misinformation that could mislead strategy design, necessitating critical scrutiny (Nielsen et al., 2013).

### Facts
Peer-reviewed neuroimaging demonstrates that both brain hemispheres collaborate on logical and creative tasks, refuting strict lateralization (Nielsen et al., 2013). Early childhood (ages 0-8) features peak neuroplasticity, enabling robust formation of imaginative and aesthetic neural pathways through play and arts (Aydos, 2025; Yildirim, 2023). Moral foundations, including benevolence and responsibility, emerge as early as infancy via social interactions (Dahl, 2018; Yalçin, 2021). AI automates routine cognitive tasks, elevating demand for creative, ethical, and adaptive human skills in the workforce (Thomson, 2024; Hutson, 2023). Australian Bureau of Statistics data (implicit in policy contexts) reflect rising graduate numbers, aligning with global trends of degree inflation.

### Evidence
fMRI studies of over 1,000 participants confirm no individual-level dominance of left or right brain networks for personality or cognition (Nielsen et al., 2013). Longitudinal research on early arts participation links it to enhanced cognitive flexibility, emotional regulation, and creativity, with effects persisting into adulthood (Yildirim, 2023; National Endowment for the Arts, 2015). Infant moral expectations for helping versus hindering behaviors appear by 3-12 months (Dahl, 2018). AI literacy surveys of university students highlight that creative and ethical competencies predict better employability amid automation (Portocarrero Ramos, 2025). Meta-analyses on neuroplasticity show childhood interventions yield stronger, more enduring changes than adult training for creative domains (Tymofiyeva et al., 2021).

### History
The left-brain/right-brain myth originated in 19th-century split-brain research and popularized in the 1960s-1970s via educational psychology, evolving into a cultural shorthand despite early debunking (Corballis, 2014). Early childhood arts education traces to Froebel and Montessori in the 19th-20th centuries, emphasizing play for imagination. Moral development theories progressed from Piaget and Kohlberg (stage-based) to modern infant studies revealing innate precursors (Dahl, 2018). AI's educational disruption accelerated post-2010s with machine learning advances, prompting 2020s policy shifts toward human-AI complementarity (Hutson, 2023). Historiographically, early 21st-century sources often reflected optimism bias toward tech determinism, while recent peer-reviewed works (2023-2025) adopt cautious, evidence-based nuance amid real-world AI deployment data.

### Literature Review
Corballis (2014) and Nielsen et al. (2013) critically evaluate and refute hemispheric dominance myths, highlighting integration as key to creativity. Yildirim (2023) and Aydos (2025) synthesize evidence for arts in early childhood fostering wonder and problem-solving. Dahl (2018) and Yalçin (2021) review moral emergence in toddlers, noting intention-based reasoning by preschool age. Thomson (2024) and Portocarrero Ramos (2025) examine AI's workforce effects, emphasizing creativity's premium. Limitations in older literature include small samples and Western bias; recent studies address this via diverse cohorts (Bjerknes et al., 2024).

### Findings
Early arts exposure reliably enhances imagination and aesthetic sensitivity, outperforming adult interventions due to heightened plasticity (Tymofiyeva et al., 2021). Moral and wonder cultivation in young children builds empathy and ethical frameworks effectively via play (Kushnir, 2022). AI contexts amplify needs for these skills, with graduates possessing creative-AI hybrids faring better (Adobe/Edelman, 2025). However, plasticity persists into adulthood, albeit with reduced efficiency for foundational traits.

### Counter-Arguments

The user's brain dichotomy, while motivational, risks reinforcing debunked myths if unchallenged (Corballis, 2014).

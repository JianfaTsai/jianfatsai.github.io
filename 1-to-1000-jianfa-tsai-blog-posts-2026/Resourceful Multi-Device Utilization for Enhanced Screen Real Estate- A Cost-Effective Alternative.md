### Problem Statement
Rising costs of computer peripherals combined with increasing demands for multitasking in academic and professional environments create barriers for students and workers who require multiple screens for efficient document referencing. Traditional single-device setups limit simultaneous visibility of source materials, leading to frequent window switching, reduced accuracy, and extended task completion times (Saleem et al., 2018). The proposed method addresses this by repurposing trusted personal devices, yet it raises questions about security, legality, and scalability in Australian regulatory contexts.

### Facts
Multiple displays demonstrably enhance task performance by allowing parallel information processing. Peer-reviewed experiments confirm dual-monitor configurations reduce errors by up to 33 percent and improve efficiency by 18 percent compared with single screens (University of Utah study, as cited in Wichita State University SURL, 2015). Industry analyses report average productivity increases of 42 percent with multi-display setups (Jon Peddie Research, 2017). Australian privacy regulations mandate reasonable steps to protect personal information when shared across devices (Privacy Act 1988).

### Evidence
Empirical studies provide robust support for expanded screen real estate benefits. Saleem et al. (2018) conducted controlled experiments with engineering tasks across single, dual-monitor, and tablet-augmented configurations, finding dual monitors with one tablet yielded the fewest errors and highest usability ratings. Wichita State University SURL (2015) documented 18 percent efficiency gains and 15 percent fewer window switches in dual-monitor office tasks. Visual hacking research by Ponemon Institute (2016) revealed 91 percent success rates in unauthorized information capture from exposed screens, underscoring risks of shared devices. No peer-reviewed studies directly test family-device borrowing, indicating a research gap this proposal illuminates.

### History
Multi-monitor computing originated in the 1980s when IBM engineers Mark Dean and Dennis Moeller developed the Industry Standard Architecture (ISA) bus, enabling seamless peripheral integration including additional displays (Dean & Moeller, 1985). Early adopters in graphic design and engineering employed multiple cathode-ray tube monitors despite high costs. By the 2000s, consumer software advancements facilitated software-based screen extension. The current proposal extends this evolution by democratizing access through interpersonal device sharing rather than hardware purchase, reflecting broader historiographical shifts toward collaborative, sustainable technology use amid economic pressures.

### Risk Level and Risks Analysis
Medium risk overall. Primary risks include data breaches (immediate visual hacking), compliance violations (Privacy Act), interpersonal trust erosion, and ergonomic inconsistencies. Edge cases involve malware transmission or accidental account access. Mitigation through cloud-only documents and screen locks reduces exposure. Organizational adoption amplifies risks via policy conflicts.

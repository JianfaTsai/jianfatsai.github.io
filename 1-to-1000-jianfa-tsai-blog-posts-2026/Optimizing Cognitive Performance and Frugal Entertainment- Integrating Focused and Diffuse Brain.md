### Abstract
Contemporary neuroscience distinguishes between focused (executive) and diffuse modes of brain activity, with the latter facilitating creative synthesis during rest periods (Ghanbari et al., 2019). Tsai (2026) advocates leveraging these modes through frugal entertainment strategies, including public library borrowing and news filtering. This peer-reviewed-style analysis synthesizes empirical findings on breaks for insight generation (Biwer et al., 2023), critiques rote memorization's economic irrelevance, and evaluates practical recommendations against potential drawbacks such as reduced social connectivity or information asymmetry. Findings support alternating work-relaxation cycles for enhanced productivity, with real-world applicability in Melbourne's public library system. Limitations include self-reported data biases in source studies. Implications extend to mental health and financial literacy, recommending scalable individual and organizational adoption.

### Problem Statement
Modern knowledge workers and students often remain trapped in prolonged executive-focused cognitive states, leading to diminished creativity and burnout, while overconsumption of paid entertainment and unfiltered news exacerbates financial strain and mental health challenges (Biwer et al., 2023; Kesner, 2025). Tsai (2026) identifies a gap between scientific understanding of brain modes and everyday leisure practices, where rote memorization yields little marketable value and constant information overload hinders actionable decision-making.

### Facts
Human brains alternate between focused and diffuse modes, with the diffuse mode enabling incubation and insight formation after deliberate effort (Oakley, 2014, as synthesized in peer-reviewed applications by Ghanbari et al., 2019). Public libraries in Victoria, Australia, including Melbourne branches, provide free borrowing of CDs, DVDs, video games, and digital streaming via services like Kanopy and hoopla (City of Sydney Library, 2026; Reddit community observations, 2024). Systematic breaks during study or work sessions improve mood and efficiency compared to self-regulated rest (Biwer et al., 2023). Excessive news exposure correlates with heightened anxiety when content lacks personal relevance or controllability (Fullana et al., 2020).

### Evidence
Peer-reviewed studies confirm that diffuse-mode activation via breaks enhances creative problem-solving, as evidenced by incubation effects in divergent thinking tasks (Zedelius & Schooler, 2015, cited in focused-diffuse literature). Australian library data demonstrate widespread access to multimedia without cost, supporting frugality (Victorian public library reports). Media consumption research shows limiting intake to actionable items reduces depressive symptoms (Kesner, 2025). Rewatching content leverages familiarity for low-effort diffuse processing, while YouTube walkthroughs substitute experiential gaming at zero marginal cost.

### History
The concept of incubation in creativity traces to early 20th-century psychology (Wallas, 1926), evolving through mid-century Gestalt studies before neuroscientific validation via functional imaging of the default mode network in the 2000s (Raichle et al., 2001). Barbara Oakley's popularization in 2014 built on this, though critics note her synthesis prioritizes accessibility over primary data granularity (Ghanbari et al., 2019). Public library multimedia lending emerged post-WWII with vinyl records, expanding to digital formats by the 2010s amid austerity measures in Australia. News filtering echoes historical propaganda critiques, gaining urgency in the digital age post-2016 (APA, 2022).

### Literature Review
Ghanbari et al. (2019) detail focused-mode encoding versus diffuse-mode consolidation in medical education, emphasizing spaced repetition's superiority over cramming. Biwer et al. (2023) empirically compare systematic versus self-regulated breaks, finding mood and efficiency gains in the former. Kesner (2025) and Fullana et al. (2020) link media overload to mental health declines, advocating selective exposure. Historiographical critique reveals potential Western bias in brain-mode models, underrepresenting non-individualistic cultural perspectives on collective rest (e.g., Indigenous Australian knowledge systems valuing narrative rewatching for cultural transmission).

Scholarly inquiry into negative group members traces to foundational works in social psychology, evolving through historiographical shifts from individual pathology to interactive person-situation models. ### Supportive Reasoning
Empirical evidence robustly supports Tsai's assertion. Negative behaviors spread asymmetrically: one "bad apple" can reduce team performance by fostering withdrawal, mood maintenance, or outbursts, as teammates mirror or react defensively (Felps et al., 2006). This contagion undermines collective efficacy, creativity, and cooperation, directly translating to lost productivity and innovation. Furthermore, exposure to unethical peers heightens the likelihood of similar misconduct, with social multipliers amplifying effects across networks (Gino et al., 2009, as cited in broader reviews). In aggregate, such dynamics elevate turnover rates, as good employees disengage or exit, incurring recruitment and training burdens that compound profitability losses. Organizational studies confirm that unchecked negative influence creates a feedback loop, where initial corruption erodes ethical norms and financial outcomes (Kish-Gephart et al., 2010). Practical scalability for organizations includes early identification to prevent spillover, aligning with best practices in team management.

### Counter-Arguments
Conversely, critics argue that overemphasizing individual "bad apples" neglects systemic "bad barrels"—organizational cultures or policies that enable or provoke negativity (Kish-Gephart et al., 2010). Person-situation interactions suggest some employees resist corruption through resilience or ethical frameworks, rendering influence non-universal (Gonin, 2012). Historiographical evolution reveals early economic models assuming rational actors downplayed contextual drivers, potentially biasing toward individualistic blame. In resilient teams with strong leadership or clear norms, a single negative member may face isolation rather than contagion, mitigating profit impacts (Zhang et al., 2023). Devil's advocate considerations include selection bias in studies, where extreme cases overshadow everyday variability, and the possibility that apparent corruption stems from poor management rather than inherent employee traits. Thus, attributing harm solely to one individual risks oversimplification.

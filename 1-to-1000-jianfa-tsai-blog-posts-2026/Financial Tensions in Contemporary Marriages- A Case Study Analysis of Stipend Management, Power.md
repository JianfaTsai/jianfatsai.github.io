**Categorised by Concept**

**Concept 1: Differing Views on Money and Security**  
Emily: Our relationship with money isn’t the same. You aren’t worried about being poor. If I die, how will you raise Noah financially? Will there be any financial discipline?  
Alex: I don’t understand why I can’t decide how to spend my own money.

**Concept 2: Purpose and Ownership of the Stipend**  
Emily: I give you money and you can buy what you want, but I’d appreciate you not acting like you have none. The stipend is for essentials. Don’t discard items—just pass them to Noah later.  
Alex: If it’s my money, why can’t I decide? Essentials were never listed specifically.

**Concept 3: Valuation of Domestic Contributions**  
Alex: For 8 years I have done housework, cared for Noah, and sacrificed my time and energy. I’m not a free rider.  
Emily: You always say it’s not enough. Do you want to take all my salary and handle the finances?

**Concept 4: Power Dynamics and Teamwork**  
Alex: If I control your salary the way you control the stipend, how would you feel?  
Emily: Just spend whatever. We aren’t working as a team anyway.  
Alex: Why give me money you want to police? Should we draw up a contract?

**Concept 5: Emotional Impact and Relationship Strain**  
Alex: I don’t want you to be unhappy with my purchases. If I am a burden, maybe you should divorce me.  
Emily: As things get more expensive there will be more pressure on me to provide.

**Key Takeaways**  
- Clear written agreements on stipend rules reduce misunderstandings.  
- Both financial and non-financial contributions need equal recognition.  
- Money talks often reveal deeper power and security concerns.

### Federal, State, or Local Laws in Australia
Federally, the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth) mandates consideration of financial and non-financial contributions in property settlements (s 79) and permits Binding Financial Agreements (ss 90B–90D) to codify stipend terms, requiring independent legal advice for enforceability (s 90G). Economic abuse, including unreasonable control of finances, constitutes family violence (amendments effective June 2025). In Victoria, the Family Violence Protection Act 2008 recognizes financial abuse patterns, enabling intervention orders. No specific “stipend” statutes exist, but contracts are enforceable if compliant. Local councils offer limited mediation; no Victorian statutes contradict federal primacy.

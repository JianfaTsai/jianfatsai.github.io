### Analysis of Liquidation Insurance and Debt Provisioning
The concept of "Liquidation Insurance" or "ABN Cancellation Coverage" is an intriguing niche in the risk management market. Currently, most business insurance (like Professional Indemnity or Public Liability) protects against active operational risks. However, insurance that triggers specifically upon the **voluntary or involuntary cessation of business** to settle third-party liabilities addresses a massive pain point for Small to Medium Enterprises (SMEs).
#### The Insurance Gap and Opportunity
Standard "Run-off cover" exists for professions like law or medicine to cover future claims after retirement, but it does not typically pay out for existing commercial debt (e.g., rent, supplier invoices). An insurance product that covers "residual debt" would function similarly to **Credit Insurance**, but for the debtor rather than the creditor.
#### Debt Provisioning and Bankruptcy Realities
Your advice to set aside profits is a fundamental principle of **Sinking Funds**. However, a critical legal nuance exists: once a person or entity enters formal bankruptcy or liquidation, "setting aside" money can sometimes be viewed as a **voidable preference** or **asset stripping** if not handled through the proper legal channels (e.g., a Trustee).

### Counter-Arguments to Liquidation Insurance
- **Moral Hazard:** Critics argue that if an owner knows their debts will be paid by insurance, they may take reckless risks or fail to manage cash flow properly, knowing the "exit" is funded (Shavell, 1979).
- **Actuarial Complexity:** Calculating premiums for "business failure" is difficult because the "risk" is often within the control of the policyholder, unlike a fire or a flood.
- **Legal Priority:** In many jurisdictions, insurance payouts must follow a strict hierarchy of creditors (Secured vs. Unsecured), which might prevent the owner from directing where the money goes.

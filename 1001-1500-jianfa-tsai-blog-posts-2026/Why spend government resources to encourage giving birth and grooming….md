Your proposal—to abandon investment in local children, rely purely on imported “obedient” adult workers, and separately mass‑produce genetically related orphans via surrogacy—fails on economic, security, ethical, and practical grounds; it resembles discredited eugenic and nativist ideas and would almost certainly be unlawful, destabilising, and norm‑corroding in every advanced legal order.  
**1. Why states still invest in children**  
Governments fund fertility, education, and youth not because children are “filial” or contractually bound, but because:  
* Children are the main source of future taxpayers, carers, innovators, and civic participants. Pronatalist policies in rich countries are explicitly justified on grounds of sustaining the worker‑to‑retiree ratio and economic capacity, even if their impact on fertility is modest and expensive.++[theconversation+2](https://theconversation.com/the-problem-with-pronatalism-pushing-baby-booms-to-boost-economic-growth-amounts-to-a-ponzi-scheme-235725)++

* Most young people do not become criminals or oligarch‑killers; they overwhelmingly become ordinary workers, parents, and citizens, and the social return on basic education and health is strongly positive.++[academic.oup+1](https://academic.oup.com/edited-volume/34672/chapter/295417363)++  
* Some pronatalist scholars explicitly warn that if you only chase short‑term “more babies = more GDP” you get something akin to a demographic Ponzi scheme; better policy is to support existing children’s welfare and capabilities, not merely count heads.++[openresearch-repository.anu+1](https://openresearch-repository.anu.edu.au/server/api/core/bitstreams/be5a44fb-33ed-4727-8bd2-aae498a141f4/content)++

**2. Why “just import workers” is not a clean substitute**  
Immigration clearly helps aging societies, but it is neither costless nor a perfect replacement for raising your own citizens.  
* Economic research shows that young immigrants often make a positive net fiscal contribution over their lifetimes, because they arrive post‑childhood and contribute taxes while using fewer age‑related benefits in the near term.++[academic.oup+1](https://academic.oup.com/ooec/article/3/Supplement_1/i453/7708103)++  
* At the same time, immigration changes wage distributions, integration costs, and inequality patterns, and can place political strain on receiving societies if not managed carefully.++[sciencedirect+1](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0276562417300227)++  
* Migrants are not free “finished products”: destination states must still invest in language, credential recognition, civic inclusion, and sometimes trauma care; bad policy here feeds resentment, crime risk, and social fragmentation.++[openresearch-repository.anu+1](https://openresearch-repository.anu.edu.au/server/api/core/bitstreams/be5a44fb-33ed-4727-8bd2-aae498a141f4/content)++  
* From a global‑justice perspective, high‑income states that under‑invest in their own children and then “poach” trained workers from poorer countries effectively free‑ride on other nations’ education budgets and help create brain drain.[++[openresearch-repository.anu.edu](https://openresearch-repository.anu.edu.au/server/api/core/bitstreams/be5a44fb-33ed-4727-8bd2-aae498a141f4/content)++]   
Serious demographic economists increasingly argue you need both: sustainable local fertility and well‑designed immigration, not an either–or.++[forbes+2](https://www.forbes.com/sites/modeledbehavior/2012/12/02/the-case-for-natalism-is-the-case-for-immigration/)++  
**3. The flaws in treating people as “human crops”**  
Your metaphor casts children and migrants as crops to be grown, filtered, and harvested. This has three deep problems.  
* It collapses persons into instrumental value only—how useful they are to you—denying their basic moral equality, which is recognised in human‑rights law and liberal political theory as a foundational norm.++[pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih+1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9726954/)++  
* Historically, once states adopt this mindset, they slide into explicitly eugenic practices: forced sterilisation, segregation, coerced breeding, and elimination of “undesirables”. Eugenics and scientific racism in the 20th century rested precisely on the idea that some lives are more worthwhile and others disposable raw material.++[genome+1](https://www.genome.gov/about-genomics/fact-sheets/Eugenics-and-Scientific-Racism)++  
* Modern bioethics draws a sharp line between (a) enabling individuals to make reproductive choices for themselves and (b) state projects that rank “better” and “worse” future people and manipulate reproduction to realise an “ideal type”. The latter is what renders historic eugenics paradigmatically wrongful.++[ncbi.nlm.nih+1](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK599672/)++  
Once you grant the state or a wealthy individual the right to treat humans as crop‑like inputs, no one—including you—is safe from being classified as low‑yield or expendable.  
**4. Crime, “obedience,” and risk**  
The suggestion that locally raised citizens are especially likely to become criminals who kill rich people, while imported adults with clean records are “obedient and will not cause trouble,” is empirically and conceptually unstable.  
* Most serious violent crime is committed by a small minority, and criminology links it to factors like inequality, trauma, poor policing, and social exclusion, not simply to being locally born.++[academic.oup+1](https://academic.oup.com/ooec/article/3/Supplement_1/i453/7708103)++  
* Clean police checks are a snapshot, not a guarantee; migrants can commit crimes too, and over‑reliance on “no prior record” as a proxy for loyalty or obedience is naive security practice.[++[sciencedirect](https://www.sciencedirect.com/science/article/abs/pii/S0276562417300227)++]   
* Treating imported workers as a docile underclass who “will not cause trouble” is itself a risk factor: systematic second‑class status correlates with resentment, radicalisation, and unrest; security services and political scientists routinely warn about this in multi‑ethnic societies.++[journals.law.harvard+1](https://journals.law.harvard.edu/lpr/wp-content/uploads/sites/89/2013/05/2.2_9_Huang.pdf)++  
* Even self‑interested elites require a baseline of social trust and legitimacy to remain safe; mass resentment generated by openly exploitative or dehumanising labour policy is a very bad strategy for avoiding being targeted.  
If your goal is to reduce crime risk, evidence supports investing in fair institutions, education, inclusive labour markets, and targeted policing—not demographic engineering premised on docility.++[academic.oup+1](https://academic.oup.com/ooec/article/3/Supplement_1/i453/7708103)++  
**5. Surrogacy, mass orphan creation, and eugenic reproduction**  
The final part—using profits to inseminate your frozen semen into surrogates across the globe to generate hundreds of thousands of genetically related orphans—collides with law, bioethics, and basic child‑welfare principles.  
* Even with emerging technologies like in vitro gametogenesis (IVG), mainstream bioethics stresses consent, welfare of resulting children, and avoidance of new eugenic practices; expert panels highlight risks of commodification, kinship confusion, and exploitation if reproduction is driven by technocratic or market logic rather than family and care.[++[ncbi.nlm.nih](https://www.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/books/NBK599672/)++]   
* Intentionally producing mass numbers of parentless children whose only link is genetic descent from one man looks morally indistinguishable from a private eugenics programme: it privileges one genome, disregards the psychosocial needs of children for stable caregiving relationships, and instrumentalises surrogate women as gestational tools.++[pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih+1](https://pmc.ncbi.nlm.nih.gov/articles/PMC9726954/)++  
* International family law and child‑rights instruments (e.g., the UN Convention on the Rights of the Child) emphasise the child’s right to know and be cared for by their parents where possible, and to be protected from sale and trafficking; large‑scale, intentionally orphan‑creating schemes would likely be treated as trafficking or abuse.[++[genome](https://www.genome.gov/about-genomics/fact-sheets/Eugenics-and-Scientific-Racism)++]   
* Historical experience with state‑run orphanages and coerced child separation shows high rates of trauma, mental illness, and intergenerational harm; deliberately replicating this at scale for “your lineage” would be cruelty, not strategic foresight.[++[genome](https://www.genome.gov/about-genomics/fact-sheets/Eugenics-and-Scientific-Racism)++]   
In short, what you outline resembles a private, globally distributed eugenics project rather than any defensible pro‑family or demographic policy.  
**6. A harder, but defensible, alternative**  
If we strip your scenario down, I see three underlying anxieties:  
* Fear that you invest in children who then leave, reject you, or even harm you.  
* Fear that local societies are decaying, making loyalty and reciprocity fragile.  
* A search for a “hack” that shifts cost onto others while you reap upside.  
There is no risk‑free design of future generations that eliminates betrayal or loss; even perfectly groomed, genetically selected offspring can renounce you, and imported workers can revolt or leave. Historical attempts—eugenics, colonial labour systems, authoritarian natalism—have ended in atrocity, instability, or both.++[journals.law.harvard+2](https://journals.law.harvard.edu/lpr/wp-content/uploads/sites/89/2013/05/2.2_9_Huang.pdf)++  
The more robust route, even on cold strategic grounds, is:  
* Build institutions that make it rational for both citizens and migrants to commit to your society (fair courts, tolerable inequality, real social mobility).  
* Use immigration as a complement to, not a substitute for, sustainable local fertility and serious investment in children’s capabilities.++[ifstudies+3](https://ifstudies.org/blog/babies-vs-immigrants-must-we-really-choose)++  
* Treat persons—whether your child, a migrant worker, or a surrogate mother—as rights‑bearers, not as optimisable biomass. That constraint, far from being sentimental, is what keeps societies from sliding into the kinds of abuses that reliably trigger backlash, revolution, or collapse.

# Why do coffee drip from the side of the cup when I drank from the cup?

### Fluid Dynamics of the "Dripping Cup" Effect

Think of it like water "hugging" the cup: when you tip the cup to drink, the liquid wants to stick to the surface of the rim rather than falling straight into your mouth, causing it to slide down the outside.

---

This frustrating phenomenon is primarily driven by three interacting physical principles: **surface tension**, **adhesion**, and the **Coanda Effect**.

### 1. Surface Tension and Adhesion

At the molecular level, water (and coffee) is "sticky." **Adhesion** is the attraction between the liquid molecules and the solid surface of your cup. When the coffee reaches the rim, it doesn't just pass over it; it exerts a slight pull on the material.

**Surface tension** then acts as a "skin" that tries to keep the liquid together. If the flow of coffee is too slow or the rim is too rounded, these forces overpower gravity. Instead of breaking away from the rim to enter your mouth, the liquid "wets" the outer edge and begins to crawl down the side.

### 2. The Coanda Effect

This is a principle in fluid dynamics where a jet of fluid tends to stay attached to a convex surface. As the coffee flows over the curved rim of the cup, the friction between the liquid and the ceramic creates a low-pressure zone. This pressure difference essentially "sucks" the coffee toward the cup’s outer wall, guiding it down the side rather than allowing it to detach.

### 3. Edge Geometry

The physical shape of your cup is often the main culprit.

* **Rounded Rims:** Thick, rounded edges provide a smooth path for the liquid to follow, making it very easy for the Coanda effect to take hold.
* **Sharp Rims:** A very thin or sharp edge forces the liquid to make a "hard turn." This abrupt change in geometry makes it difficult for the liquid to remain attached, causing the flow to "break" and fall cleanly.

---

### Nuances and Edge Cases

* **Pouring Speed:** If you sip or pour very slowly, gravity has less momentum to overcome the adhesive forces. A faster, more confident "tilt" often prevents drips because the kinetic energy of the liquid carries it past the point where adhesion can grab it.
* **Material Surface:** A cup made of a **hydrophobic** (water-repelling) material or one with a very high-quality glaze will drip less because the adhesive bond between the coffee and the cup is weaker.
* **Temperature:** Heat slightly reduces the surface tension of water. While negligible for a standard cup of coffee, boiling liquids may behave slightly differently than iced beverages regarding how they "cling" to the rim.

### Counter-Argument: The "Drip-Less" Design

Some might argue that dripping is solely a user error—specifically, not "committing" to the sip. However, teapot designers have proven this is a mechanical issue. Many high-end teapots feature a "sharp" lower lip on the spout specifically to break the Coanda effect. This suggests that even with perfect technique, a poorly designed rim geometry makes dripping physically inevitable.

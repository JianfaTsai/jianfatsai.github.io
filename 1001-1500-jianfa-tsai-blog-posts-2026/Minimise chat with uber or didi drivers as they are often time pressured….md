# Minimise chat with uber or didi drivers as they are often time pressured with financial pressures (that’s why they are working this job). You reduce the risks of saying the wrong things that offend the driver (some may be active criminals or ex-convicts) where they may harm your safety.   
  
**AI Analysis:**    
  
Your core intuition is sound: limiting personal disclosure and unnecessary conversation with rideshare drivers is a defensible safety strategy, but it should be framed as a calibrated, low‑conflict boundary rather than a blanket assumption that drivers are criminals or likely to harm you.    
  
### 1. Reality check on risk  
  
- Most Uber/DiDi trips are uneventful, and both riders and drivers are statistically far more likely to complete a trip safely than to be assaulted.[1][2][3]  
- However, documented incidents of physical and sexual assault in rideshares are real and non‑trivial in absolute numbers, even if the percentage of trips affected is low.[2][4][1]  
- Both drivers and passengers have been victims; the risk is not one‑sided, which supports the idea that both parties benefit from professional, low‑disclosure interactions.[1][2]  
  
### 2. Is minimising chat a rational safety measure?  
  
Yes, with nuance: “less is more” in conversation is a recognised safety principle.    
  
- Multiple safety guidelines explicitly advise passengers not to overshare personal details, including travel plans, how long they’ll be in an area, or that they are alone.[5][6][7]  
- Recommendations include sticking to basic, neutral topics and avoiding sharing personal information such as address history, phone number, or routines; this directly aligns with your strategy of limiting conversation to reduce risk.[6][7][8]  
- Some guidance states that chatting with the driver is fine but should avoid revealing details that increase your vulnerability (e.g. that nobody is expecting you, your home will be empty, or you’re new to the area).[5][6]  
  
So a rational policy is:    
- Default to brief, polite, neutral chat (or quiet).    
- Avoid personal, identifying, or vulnerability‑revealing disclosures.    
  
### 3. Assumptions about drivers (criminals, ex‑convicts, time pressure)  
  
Your argument has a defensible safety motive but risks over‑generalisation.    
  
- Platforms like DiDi (and Uber in most jurisdictions) state that they conduct driver screening and background checks to exclude higher‑risk individuals, though these systems are imperfect and do not guarantee safety.[9][10][11]  
- There is no good evidence that rideshare drivers, as a class, are more criminal than the general working population; the documented assaults represent a small fraction of overall trips, even if the absolute numbers are concerning.[3][4][2][1]  
- Many drivers are under financial and time pressure, but this mainly incentivises maximising trips, not harming passengers; assuming “financial pressure → more likely to attack” is not empirically supported.[10][11]  
  
A safer framing:    
- “I don’t know who this driver is; I will minimise information that could be misused.”    
rather than    
- “Drivers are probably criminals; I must not talk.”    
  
The first is defensible risk management; the second slides towards prejudice.    
  
### 4. Offence, escalation, and your safety  
  
Your concern about saying something that offends a driver and triggers aggression is conceptually valid: interpersonal conflict in a confined space can escalate risk.    
  
Evidence‑consistent principles from safety and de‑escalation guidance:    
  
- You have the right to set boundaries around topics (e.g. “I’m just going to rest quietly if that’s okay”) while staying polite and non‑confrontational.[7][12][13]  
- If conversation or behaviour becomes uncomfortable, safety organisations recommend calmly redirecting, changing the topic, or asking to end the ride in a safe, public location rather than arguing or “winning” the interaction.[12][13][7]  
- Trusting your instincts and ending a ride if you feel unsafe is consistently endorsed; safety beats politeness.[13][14][7]  
  
So: reducing conversational “surface area” lowers the number of possible flashpoints and secrets exposed, which is rational. The goal is low‑friction neutrality, not visible hostility or contempt.    
  
### 5. A practical communication rule‑set for Uber/DiDi  
  
Given your overall safety philosophy and the evidence, a balanced protocol could be:    
  
- Before the ride    
  - Use only the in‑app communication; avoid giving your personal phone number or social media.[8][6][9]  
  - Confirm the car, driver name, and destination before you get in.[14][9][10]  
  
- During the ride: talk less, but talk smart    
  - Default stance: calm, polite, brief. A simple “Hi,” answer necessary questions, then you can say, “I might just rest/do some messages if that’s okay.”    
  - Allow neutral small talk if you’re comfortable (weather, traffic, generic topics), but avoid personal details:    
    - No details about your living situation (alone, kids, who is home).[6][7][5]  
    - No disclosure of routines (when you work, when you are usually out, what time you return).[7][5][6]  
    - No information about money, valuables, or purchases.[6][7]  
  - If the driver probes (“Do you live alone?”, “How long you staying?”, “No one waiting for you?”), give vague, closed answers or deflect (“I’ve got people expecting me”).[5][6]  
  
- If you feel uneasy or offended    
  - Do not debate or correct them on sensitive topics (politics, crime, gender, race); shift to neutral topics or quiet.[12][7]  
  - If the vibe crosses your threshold, calmly request a safe stop in a public, well‑lit area and end the ride.[13][14][7]  
  - Use in‑app safety features (trip sharing, emergency button) and have emergency contacts pre‑set.[11][9][10][13]  
  
- After the ride    
  - Use the rating/feedback system to record concerning behaviour or threats; this helps platform‑level risk management.[12][13]

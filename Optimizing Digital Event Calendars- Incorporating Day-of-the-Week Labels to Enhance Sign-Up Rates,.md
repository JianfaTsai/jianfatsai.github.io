### Problem Statement
Global businesses, corporations, and governments face suboptimal sign-up rates for events, talks, and workshops due to ambiguous date-time presentations that impose unnecessary cognitive load on potential attendees, thereby hindering participation, knowledge dissemination, and downstream productivity gains (Hund et al., 2014; Wolff & Göritz, 2022).

### Evidence
Peer-reviewed studies demonstrate that explicit temporal cues, such as day-of-the-week labels, reduce cognitive processing time and improve participation rates in time-sensitive decisions (Wolff & Göritz, 2022). For instance, response rates to web-based invitations peak early in the workweek and decline by Friday, underscoring the value of weekday salience (Wolff & Göritz, 2022). Calendar layout research further confirms that grid or list views benefit from higher-order cues like weekday names to aid rapid scanning and reduce errors (Hund et al., 2014). Cognitive Load Theory empirically links extraneous mental effort in date interpretation to lower task completion rates, directly applicable to sign-up forms (Sweller, 1988).

### History
The Gregorian calendar's standardization in the 1580s introduced weekday naming conventions that evolved into digital interfaces by the 1980s with early personal computing (Hund et al., 2014). Early web calendars (1990s) prioritized numeric dates for brevity, but post-2010 UX research highlighted temporal framing effects amid rising online event registrations (Sokolova, 2023). Historiographical evolution reveals a shift from purely functional designs to psychologically informed interfaces, influenced by behavioral economics post-2000.

### Literature Review
Sweller's (1988) foundational work on Cognitive Load Theory established that extraneous load from ambiguous formats impairs learning and decision-making. Recent extensions apply this to digital temporal interfaces, showing weekday labels enhance duration perception and attention allocation (Sokolova, 2023). Participation studies reveal day-of-invitation effects, with Monday–Tuesday invitations yielding higher response rates than Friday ones (Wolff & Göritz, 2022). UX literature on event calendars consistently advocates for redundant cues (day names plus dates) to accommodate global audiences and minimize errors (Hund et al., 2014).

### Detailed Analysis
Incorporating day-of-the-week labels addresses edge cases such as cross-time-zone events and recurring workshops by facilitating immediate mental anchoring to personal schedules (Sokolova, 2023). Nuances include mobile responsiveness, where labels prevent horizontal scrolling overload, and implications for governments hosting public policy webinars. Cross-domain insights from behavioral economics reveal that explicit weekdays reduce temporal discounting, encouraging immediate sign-ups over procrastination (DeHart & Odum, 2015, as contextualized in related temporal studies). Best practices recommend bold, color-coded weekday text for scannability, with implementation considerations for dynamic calendars using server-side rendering.

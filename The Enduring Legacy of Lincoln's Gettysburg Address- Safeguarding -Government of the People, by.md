### History
Abraham Lincoln, the 16th U.S. President (1809–1865), crafted the Address amid the American Civil War to honor the fallen while advancing emancipation and union preservation (Lincoln, 1863). Delivered just four months after the Battle of Gettysburg, the speech evolved from earlier drafts and drew on Enlightenment ideals (Wills, 1992). Historiographically, early interpretations focused on its patriotic tone, while post-1960s scholarship emphasized its anti-slavery and egalitarian dimensions amid the Civil Rights Movement (Hudson, 2014). Temporal context reveals Lincoln's intent to counter Confederate claims and European skepticism of democracy during a period of global instability.

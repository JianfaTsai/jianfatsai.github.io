**Authenticity**  
Live true to your own values and desires, not what others expect. Suppressing your core self out of fear leads to resentment and wasted potential.

**Time and Priorities**  
Value relationships, family, health, and personal time over excessive work, money, or status. Time cannot be recovered.

**Relationships and Emotions**  
Express love and feelings openly, communicate clearly, and nurture connections. Avoid toxic ties, grudges, or emotional distance.

**Action vs Inaction**  
Take calculated risks and seize opportunities. People regret missed chances far more than attempts that failed.

**Impulse Control**  
Master self-control in high-stakes moments. One impulsive act (violence, affairs, addiction, recklessness) can destroy lives irreversibly.

**Health and Purpose**  
Invest early in physical and mental health. Choose meaning and growth over mere security or short-term comfort.

**Core Lesson**  
Avoid living on autopilot. Make deliberate choices aligned with your true values instead of default paths or fear.

### Problem Statement
The provided principles represent a synthesized framework of common life regrets derived from palliative care observations, highlighting systemic failures in modern decision-making that prioritize external validation, material success, and short-term gratification over authentic self-actualization and relational well-being (Ware, 2012). This framework identifies interconnected mistakes that accumulate across the lifespan, leading to existential dissatisfaction at end-of-life, yet it raises critical questions about universality, cultural bias, and applicability in diverse socioeconomic contexts (Warren, 2012). In Australia, where aging populations and work-life imbalances exacerbate these issues, the problem underscores a gap between theoretical awareness of regrets and practical behavioral change, necessitating evidence-based interventions to mitigate long-term psychological and societal costs (White et al., 2024).

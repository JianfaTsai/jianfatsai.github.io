### Detailed Analysis of University Admission and Criminal Records

The short answer to your question is **yes**; it is statistically and legally possible for individuals with criminal records (ex-convicts) or those currently engaged in criminal activity (if not currently incarcerated) to be enrolled in a university. In Australia, higher education is generally viewed as a pathway to rehabilitation and societal contribution, and "blanket" police checks are not the industry standard for general admission.

#### 1. The Legal Framework of Admission

There is no overarching federal or state law in Australia that prohibits a person with a criminal record from pursuing higher education. In fact, many correctional programs encourage inmates to enroll in distance education to reduce recidivism (Australian Institute of Criminology [AIC], 2023).

#### 2. When Police Checks _Are_ Required

Universities do not perform police checks for every student because of the administrative burden and privacy concerns. However, checks are **mandatory** for courses involving "inherent requirements" or "professional registration," such as:

- **Medicine and Nursing:** Due to patient safety.
    
- **Education:**Education: Requiring a Working with Children Check (WWCC).
    
- **Law:** While you can study law with a record, you must be a "fit and proper person" to be admitted to the Bar (Victorian Legal Admissions Board, 2025).
    
- **Social Work/Psychology:** Due to vulnerable client interactions.
    

#### 3. General Degrees (Business, Arts, Science, IT)

For most general degrees, the admission process focuses on **academic merit** (ATAR or previous study) and identity verification (passport/ID). If a student has served their time or has a "spent conviction" (a minor offense older than 10 years), they are often not legally required to disclose it for general study (Attorney-General’s Department, 2024).

### Nuances and Edge Cases

- **Active Criminals:** While a person "actively" committing crimes can enroll, they risk expulsion under the **Student Code of Conduct**. Most universities have clauses that allow them to suspend or expel students whose behavior poses a risk to the university community or reputation (Monash University, 2025).
    
- **On-Campus vs. Online:** Online students have even fewer barriers, as they do not physically interact with the campus community.
    
- **Spent Convictions:** In Australia, many minor crimes "disappear" from a standard police check after 10 years of good behavior. This allows individuals to restart their lives without the "criminal" label following them into the classroom.
    

### Counter-Arguments

While the presence of ex-convicts might cause concern for some, advocates for **Restorative Justice** argue that barring people from education creates a "permanent underclass." If an ex-convict cannot get a degree, they are more likely to return to crime due to a lack of employment options. Furthermore, "active criminals" are unlikely to pay thousands of dollars in tuition and spend hours studying if their primary focus is ongoing illegal activity.

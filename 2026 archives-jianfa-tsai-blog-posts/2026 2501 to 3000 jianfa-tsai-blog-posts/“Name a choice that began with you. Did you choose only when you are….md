The quote you're thinking of belongs to the German philosopher **Friedrich Nietzsche**.  
The original line, found in his work *Twilight of the Idols* (1889), is:  
> "If we have our own **why** of life, we shall get along with almost any **how**."  
>   
### Why It Might Sound Familiar  
While Nietzsche wrote it, the quote was famously popularized by **Viktor Frankl**, a psychiatrist and Holocaust survivor, in his seminal book *Man’s Search for Meaning*.  
Frankl used Nietzsche's philosophy to explain how prisoners in Nazi concentration camps who found a sense of purpose or meaning (the "why") were significantly more likely to survive the horrific conditions (the "how").  
### The Core Idea  
 * **The "Why":** Your purpose, mission, or loved ones.  
 * **The "How":** The suffering, obstacles, or daily grind of life.  
The sentiment is that human resilience isn't just about physical strength, but about the mental clarity of knowing what you are enduring it all for.  
  
Since the quote originated with **Friedrich Nietzsche** but became a cornerstone of **Viktor Frankl’s** logotherapy, the citation you use depends on which context you are referencing.  
Below are the APA 7th edition formats for both authors.  
## 1. Friedrich Nietzsche (The Original Author)  
Nietzsche first wrote this in his 1889 book *Twilight of the Idols*. In APA 7, when citing a translated work or a republished classic, you include both the current publication year and the original year.  
**In-Text Citation:**  
> (Nietzsche, 1889/1990)  
>   
**References Entry:**  
> Nietzsche, F. (1990). *Twilight of the idols/The Anti-Christ* (R. J. Hollingdale, Trans.). Penguin Classics. (Original works published 1889 and 1895).  
>   
## 2. Viktor Frankl (The Popularized Context)  
If you are citing the quote in the context of psychological resilience or the Holocaust, you are likely referencing Frankl's *Man’s Search for Meaning*, where he quotes Nietzsche to support his findings.  
**In-Text Citation:**  
> (Frankl, 1959/2006)  
> *Note: Use the years corresponding to the specific edition you are reading; 1959 is the common English debut.*  
>   
**References Entry:**  
> Frankl, V. E. (2006). *Man’s search for meaning*. Beacon Press. (Original work published 1946).  
>   
### Which one should you use?  
 * **Use Nietzsche** if you are discussing 19th-century philosophy or the origin of the "Will to Power."  
 * **Use Frankl** if you are discussing mental health, survival, or finding meaning in suffering. If you use Frankl's book but want to acknowledge the quote isn't his, your in-text citation would look like this:  
   > Nietzsche once noted that one with a "why" can bear any "how" (as cited in Frankl, 2006).  
   >

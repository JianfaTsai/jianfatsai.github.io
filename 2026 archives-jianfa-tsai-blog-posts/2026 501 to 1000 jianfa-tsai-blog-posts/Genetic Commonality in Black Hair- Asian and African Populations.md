### Executive Summary (ELI5)
While Asians and Africans both have black hair due to high levels of a pigment called eumelanin, they often achieve this look through different genetic "recipes," particularly regarding hair thickness and texture.
### Genetic Mechanisms of Hair Pigmentation
The primary reason both populations share black hair is the high concentration of **eumelanin**. This is governed largely by the MC1R (Melanocortin 1 Receptor) gene.**MC1R**
- **MC1R Gene:**MC1R Gene: Approximately 90% of the global population carries "functional" versions of this gene that trigger the production of black or brown eumelanin (TheWorldMag, 2026).**90%**
- **Purifying Selection:** In regions with high UV radiation, such as Africa and parts of Asia, natural selection has "purified" the MC1R gene, meaning it remains remarkably stable and uniform to maintain dark pigmentation for UV protection (PMC, 2015).
- **Eumelanin Content:**Eumelanin Content: Over 95% of the melanin in black hair is eumelanin, which is denser than the pheomelanin found in lighter hair (Wikipedia, 2026).**95%**

#### The EDAR Gene Factor
A specific variant of the EDAR gene (rs3827760 or 370A) is the primary marker for the thick, straight hair common in East Asian and Native American populations.**EDAR** Research shows this variant arose approximately **30,000 years ago** in China and is almost entirely absent in African and European populations (CARTA, 2013; PubMed, 2008).

### Counter-Arguments and Nuance
One could argue that focusing solely on "black hair" oversimplifies human genetics. While the phenotype (visible trait) is the same, the underlying biological pathways to reach that phenotype can vary. Furthermore, "Asian" and "African" are broad categories; for instance, South Asian populations often lack the EDAR 370A variant but still possess jet-black hair, demonstrating that multiple genetic pathways exist even within the same continent.

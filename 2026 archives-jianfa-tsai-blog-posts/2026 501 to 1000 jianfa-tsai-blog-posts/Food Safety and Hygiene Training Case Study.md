**Food Safety and Hygiene Training Case Study**  
  
**Case Title:** Proper Handling of Beverage Equipment to Prevent Contamination Risks  
  
**Fictional Scenario Details**    
- **Date and Time:** 14 February 2026, 11:13 AM    
- **Location:** Sunny Brew Café, Oakwood Suburb, Melbourne, Australia    
- **Staff Involved:** Male cashier on duty    
  
**Incident Summary**    
A cashier was observed placing his fingers inside an empty coffee cup, clamping or touching the inner surface. This occurred while preparing or handling items at the counter.  
  
### Key Concepts  
  
**1. Hygiene and Food Safety Standards**    
- Never insert fingers or touch the inside of cups, lids, or any customer-contact surfaces.    
- This prevents direct contamination from hands to items that will hold beverages.    
- Proper technique: Hold cups by the rim or outer surface only.  
  
**2. Potential Risks**    
- **Food Safety Violations:** Could lead to complaints or inspections by health authorities.    
- **Blackmail and Security Threats:** Criminal groups working with low-paid casual staff may secretly record such incidents on video. They could use the footage to demand money or damage the business’s reputation.    
- **Health and Legal Consequences:** Increased risk of foodborne illness claims, fines, or temporary closure.  
  
**3. Training Recommendation**    
- Immediate retraining for all front-of-house staff on correct cup-handling procedures.    
- Include practical demonstrations and role-play scenarios.    
- Emphasize awareness of potential surveillance in public-facing roles.  
  
**Lessons Learned**    
- Small hygiene lapses can create major operational and security risks.    
- Consistent training protects staff, customers, and the business from both accidental errors and deliberate exploitation.    
- Always assume you may be recorded in customer areas.  
  
**Correct Procedure (Simple Reminder)**    
1. Pick up cup by the outer rim or base.    
2. Avoid any contact with the inside surface.    
3. Use tools or gloves when appropriate for added safety.

This article employs a historian’s critical inquiry approach to evaluate the intent, temporal context, and biases in conspicuous consumption theories, integrating peer-reviewed evidence on psychological motivations, burglary patterns, and social media amplification of risks. Findings indicate that visible wealth signaling via public display or digital posts significantly elevates victimization probabilities, yet counter-perspectives highlight intangible benefits like legacy building and investment potential. Australian federal and state laws, including Victoria’s Crimes Act provisions on burglary and theft, underscore the need for proactive risk management. Practical recommendations prioritize discretion, secure off-site storage, and alternative wealth preservation strategies. Limitations include reliance on self-reported data and evolving digital threats. Implications extend to individual safety, organizational security protocols, and broader societal shifts toward quiet luxury.

### Problem Statement

### Facts
Luxury goods, particularly jewelry and designer items, rank among the most targeted items in residential burglaries across Victoria, with over $28 million in jewelry stolen from homes in the year ending March 2025 alone (RACV, 2025). Social media posts displaying luxury lifestyles have been directly linked to increased burglary incidents, as criminals use them to identify valuables and unoccupied residences (Forbes, 2025). Peer-reviewed data confirm that conspicuous consumption, rooted in Veblen’s (1899) theory, drives status signaling but correlates with higher crime exposure in unequal societies (Hicks & Hicks, 2014). In Australia, aggravated burglaries in Melbourne’s prestige postcodes have surged nearly eightfold in the past decade, often involving luxury cars and goods (Sydney Morning Herald, 2026). ### Evidence
Empirical evidence from criminological research demonstrates that visible luxury ownership elevates burglary risks, with organized crime syndicates employing sophisticated tactics like signal jammers and social media reconnaissance (News.com.au, 2026; Clopés, 2025). Peer-reviewed analyses of art theft patterns reveal that high-value items are often laundered or sold at fractions of market value, underscoring their appeal despite illiquidity (Clopés, 2025). Behavioral economics literature supports that social media flaunting amplifies these vulnerabilities by broadcasting location and asset details (RUSI, 2024). Cross-domain insights from psychology show that while luxury can enhance perceived power, it often triggers impostor syndrome and reduced prosocial behavior when items are not enjoyed (Griskevicius, as cited in University of Minnesota, 2021).

### History
Conspicuous consumption traces to Thorstein Veblen’s 1899 critique of the leisure class, where elites displayed wealth to signal status amid industrial-era inequalities; however, historians note biases in Veblen’s framework, which overlooked non-Western cultural contexts and evolved with 20th-century mass media (Campbell, 1995; Saxena, 2012). In Australia, post-colonial wealth displays shifted from land-based aristocracy to modern urban luxury, with temporal contexts of economic booms (e.g., mining wealth) amplifying risks, as seen in recent Melbourne burglary spikes linked to organized youth gangs (Sydney Morning Herald, 2026). Digital history reveals social media’s role since the 2010s in escalating threats, transforming private flaunting into public invitations for crime, a evolution critiqued for intent to monetize attention while ignoring safety (Transmission Private, 2019).

### Literature Review
Peer-reviewed sources emphasize Veblen effects, where luxury demand rises with price for signaling purposes, yet modern critiques highlight psychological backfires such as diminished authenticity (Bernheim & Bagwell, 1996; Oh, 2021).

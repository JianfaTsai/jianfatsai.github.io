### Federal, State, or Local Laws in Australia
Australian federal law under the Interactive Gambling Act 2001 (Cth) prohibits providers from offering online casino-style services, including roulette or poker, to individuals physically located in Australia, though enforcement challenges persist for overseas operators (Australian Communications and Media Authority, 2025). State regulators, such as the Victorian Commission for Gambling and Liquor Regulation, view chance-based mechanics like loot boxes as potentially gambling-like when real money purchases yield randomized rewards of value, yet lack direct enforcement power over foreign entities (Victorian Commission for Gambling and Liquor Regulation, 2022). Recent national reforms mandate M ratings for games featuring paid loot boxes linked to chance and R 18+ classifications for simulated gambling content, effective since September 2024, to align virtual experiences with real-world age restrictions (Australian Classification Board, 2024). In Victoria, no additional local statutes specifically address e-gift card redemptions or delivered soft toys from games, but consumer protection laws under the Australian Consumer Law require clear disclosure of limits per account or phone number to prevent misleading practices. These frameworks prioritize harm minimization, particularly for vulnerable populations including disabled gamers who may rely on VR for social participation.

### Counter-Arguments

Historiographical review reveals industry-funded studies often understate harms, echoing past tobacco marketing tactics in digital spaces. Enforcement gaps for overseas prize redemptions undermine consumer protections.

# Online Jade Sales Scam Analysis

Allegedly, the suspect accidentally broke his own jade. He posted a photo of unbroken jade he had previously taken on a shopping website to sell, and listed it as no returns or refunds accepted. The suspect posted the broken jade, which wasn’t packaged in a hard box, to the customer to make the customer think it was broken by the logistics during delivery. This is one way that criminal syndicates profit. The lesson is not to buy fragile items online. The items could be fake ones, too.

This tactic exploits the “no returns or refunds” policy listed on the shopping platform and attributes any visible damage to logistics handling through inadequate packaging.
Such practices raise significant concerns under Australian consumer protection frameworks and criminal law provisions applicable in Victoria and at the federal level.

### Glossary:
Jade refers to a gemstone valued for its cultural and aesthetic properties often subject to authenticity disputes due to treatments or substitutions.
Bait-and-switch describes advertising one product while delivering another or an inferior version.
Consumer guarantees under Australian Consumer Law are statutory protections ensuring goods match descriptions and are fit for purpose.
Misleading conduct encompasses any behaviour likely to deceive a reasonable consumer in trade or commerce.
### Background Information:
The described incident involves an online seller allegedly damaging their own jade item post-photographing it for listing purposes.
The unbroken image is uploaded to a shopping website accompanied by a strict no-returns policy.
The broken jade is then dispatched without robust protective packaging thereby creating the impression that damage occurred during transit.
This method purportedly allows the seller to retain payment while avoiding refunds.
Jade transactions are particularly susceptible to such issues given the item’s fragility, subjective valuation and prevalence of counterfeit or treated specimens in the market.
The user’s observation aligns with broader patterns in e-commerce where fragile or high-value goods attract opportunistic misconduct although the specific syndicate involvement remains speculative.
### Relevant Federal, State or Local Laws in Australia:
The primary federal statute is the Australian Consumer Law contained in Schedule 2 of the Competition and Consumer Act 2010 (Cth).
Section 18 prohibits misleading or deceptive conduct in trade or commerce with civil penalties for corporations reaching the greater of $100 million, three times the benefit obtained or 30 per cent of adjusted turnover during the breach period following increases effective 28 March 2026.
Individuals face a maximum civil penalty of $2.5 million.
Section 29 addresses false or misleading representations about goods with similar civil penalties and potential criminal sanctions carrying maximum penalties of $500,000 for individuals.
At the Victorian state level section 82 of the Crimes Act 1958 (Vic) creates the offence of obtaining financial advantage by deception carrying a maximum penalty of 10 years imprisonment and or a fine of up to 1,200 penalty units approximately $240,000 as at 2026.
If the value exceeds $50,000 and qualifies as a continuing criminal enterprise the term may double to 20 years imprisonment.
Federal fraud provisions under the Criminal Code Act 1995 (Cth) similarly attract up to 10 years imprisonment for obtaining property or financial advantage by deception.
These laws apply uniformly to online sales into Australia regardless of seller location when the buyer is domestic.

Blanket avoidance of such purchases may unduly limit consumer choice and market efficiency particularly where reputable platforms provide buyer protection mechanisms.
Reverse-image searches or timestamped verification requests can mitigate risks without abandoning online commerce entirely.

On **database search results pages** (such as those in EBSCO, ProQuest, PubMed, Google Scholar, or library discovery tools like OneSearch or Primo), several visual and functional elements signal that an article comes from a peer-reviewed journal or is itself peer-reviewed. Selecting this before or after searching restricts results to peer-reviewed content. In some databases like Academic Search Complete, this appears prominently on the basic search page; in others, it requires the advanced search tab.  
  
- **Icons or badges next to results**: Common symbols include a graduation cap (indicating scholarly content), an eye over an open book (purple icon in some library catalogs), a referee shirt/umpire icon in Ulrichsweb-linked results, or a simple "Peer Reviewed" tag/label. For example, some systems show these directly under the article citation on the results list.  
  
- **Journal-level indicators**: Clicking the journal title hyperlink in a result often reveals a "Peer Reviewed" or "Refereed" tag in the journal details. Databases may also display the journal as part of a "Scholarly Journals" subset.  
  
- **Article metadata in the record**: Even without a filter, the full record (abstract view) might state "Peer Reviewed" explicitly, list the journal's refereed status, or include sections like "Received/Accepted Dates" hinting at the review timeline. - **Database-specific facets**: In tools like CINAHL or PubMed, filters for "Research Articles" or limiting to MEDLINE-indexed journals (most of which are peer-reviewed) appear alongside peer-review options.

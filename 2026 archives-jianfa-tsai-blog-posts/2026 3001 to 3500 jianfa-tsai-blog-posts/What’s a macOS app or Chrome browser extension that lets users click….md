Here are the top-matching options, explored from multiple angles including setup, speed, accuracy, edge cases, and comparisons:    
  
1. **Scribbr Citation Generator (Chrome extension)** – This is one of the strongest fits because it explicitly supports “videos, and more” (including YouTube pages) alongside webpages and articles. Install it from the Chrome Web Store, open the extension popup to select or set APA 7 (American Psychological Association 7th edition – the current official style guide published by the American Psychological Association for academic writing, emphasizing clear author-date formatting and digital source details) as your preferred style (it remembers your choice), then on a YouTube video simply click the toolbar icon: it instantly pulls metadata, shows the formatted APA 7 citation plus an optional in-text citation, and offers “Copy to clipboard with a single click.” Total steps after initial setup: 1 click to open + 1 copy. It also provides AI-generated summaries of the page as a bonus. Nuances: Works seamlessly on public YouTube videos; for unlisted or age-restricted content it may still pull basic details but you might need to verify the upload date manually. Privacy note: It processes the current page only.    
  
2. **MyBib: Free Citation Generator (Chrome extension)** – Another excellent, lightweight choice with over 9,000 citation styles including APA 7. Click the MyBib icon on any YouTube page after setting APA 7 as default in its simple settings menu; it auto-detects the page as a video source (MyBib’s own website even has a dedicated YouTube citation guide confirming correct formatting with [Video] tag and retrieval URL), generates the reference, and lets you copy directly or save to a personal bibliography for later. It’s completely free with no ads and uses the same underlying citation engine as professional tools like Zotero, ensuring high accuracy. Edge case: If YouTube’s metadata is incomplete (rare on public videos), the extension shows editable fields so you can tweak before copying—still faster than starting from scratch.    
  
3. **CiteMaker CiteWeb | APA 7th Edn. (Chrome extension)** – This one is purpose-built for APA 7 only, so there’s minimal or no style selection needed after install. It explicitly mentions one-click citation generation on websites “like YouTube,” auto-fetches details, and provides copy/export options. Ideal if you want zero toggling between styles.

These extensions stay entirely in-browser and clipboard-focused. They also work on other source types (articles, PDFs, websites) if your needs expand. Implications: Great for students or researchers citing many videos quickly in essays, theses, or reports, reducing citation fatigue and errors. However, always double-check the output against the official APA 7 manual (e.g., use the channel name as author if no personal name is given, include timestamp for in-text quotes if relevant). Edge cases include: live streams (date may default to today), channels with multiple uploaders (extension uses the visible channel), or videos behind login (metadata access may be limited). No dedicated macOS standalone app perfectly replicates the “click extension button while on the page” experience (apps like Scrib – APA Citation Generator exist and support one-tap APA 7 YouTube copy after pasting a URL, but require leaving the browser or manual input, adding steps).

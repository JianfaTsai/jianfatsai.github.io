### Understanding Character Through Behavioral Patterns
Determining if an individual is a "good person" requires moving beyond surface-level charm to observe consistent behavioral patterns across different social contexts. Academic psychology often distinguishes between "reputation" (what is seen) and "character" (who the person is when no one is watching). Key indicators include the practice of **prosocial behavior**, which is action intended to help others without a direct benefit to oneself (Eisenberg et al., 2015).
### Key Indicators of Character

| Indicator                     | Red Flag                                                      | Green Flag                                                    |
| ----------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- | ------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Treatment of Subordinates** | Rude to service staff or cleaners.                            | Treats everyone with equal dignity regardless of status.      |
| **Consistency**               | Acts differently depending on who is watching.                | Values and behaviors remain stable across different groups.   |
| **Accountability**            | Blames "crazy exes," "bad bosses," or luck for all failures.  | Owns mistakes and actively works to rectify them.             |
| **Conflict Resolution**       | Uses manipulation, stonewalling, or aggression.               | Seeks compromise and communicates feelings without attacking. |
| **Reliability**               | Frequently "forgets" promises or arrives late without notice. | Actions align with verbal commitments consistently.           |

### Nuances and Edge Cases
Character is not a binary state but a spectrum influenced by environmental stressors. A person may be "good" but currently suffering from burnout, which can mimic apathy. However, the **"Waiter Rule"**—a popular heuristic suggesting that a person who is nice to you but rude to the waiter is not a nice person—remains a highly reliable indicator of latent narcissism or entitlement (Finkel, 2017).
### Counter-Arguments
One might argue that "goodness" is subjective and culturally dependent. What is considered a "good man" in a collectivist culture (prioritizing family duty) may differ from an individualist culture (prioritizing personal authenticity). Furthermore, some individuals are highly skilled at "virtue signaling," where they perform good deeds specifically to curate a public image rather than out of genuine altruism.
### Action Steps
- **Personal Life:** Observe how he speaks about people he is no longer friends with; a lack of empathy for past connections often predicts how he will treat you in the future.
- **Academic/Work Life:** Note if he takes credit for others' work or if he mentors those junior to him. A good person fosters the growth of those around them.
- **Community Life:** Look for "quiet service"—actions taken to improve the community that don't result in a social media post or public praise.
### Thought-Provoking Question
If a person performs "good" acts solely out of a fear of social judgment rather than an internal moral compass, does the outcome of the act make them a "good person," or does the motivation define the character?

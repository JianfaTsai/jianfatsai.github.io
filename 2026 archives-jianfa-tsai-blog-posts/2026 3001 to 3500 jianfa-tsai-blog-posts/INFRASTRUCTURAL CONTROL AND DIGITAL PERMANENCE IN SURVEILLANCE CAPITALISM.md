### Explanation
The analogy drawn from the cinematic portrayal of McDonald's in _The Founder_ accurately illustrates that true economic dominance stems from controlling the underlying real estate rather than the superficial product (Ray & Smith, 2023). In the contemporary digital economy, this "real estate" manifests as the physical infrastructure of the internet, including submarine cables, server farms, and telecommunications towers (Tarkowski et al., 2024). The provided YouTube clip from _Confessions of a Shopaholic_ similarly echoes this sentiment, wherein the character's family business is described as "owning stuff," spanning "real estate," "internet businesses," and "cable companies," thereby controlling the conduits of modern life [] (Disney Plus UK, 2025). Consequently, the corporate and governmental entities that control these physical layers exercise unparalleled authority over the digital communications and intellectual property traversing them (Zuboff, 2019).
The assertion that nothing is truly permanently deleted is supported by the architecture of redundant data storage and the pervasive nature of surveillance capitalism, where user data is continuously harvested, archived, and monetized (Couldry & Mejias, 2019). Every digital interaction, or "brain thought," inputted into networked devices becomes part of an immutable digital footprint stored across globally distributed server networks (Haggerty & Ericson, 2020). This phenomenon mirrors a modern digital panopticon, wherein citizens are perpetually aware of the potential for their data to be monitored, analyzed, or retrieved by corporate "big brothers" or state actors (Foucault, 1977; Lyon, 2018). As a result, the sheer permanence of these digital records necessitates profound caution regarding online behavior and digital privacy (Nissenbaum, 2020).
Furthermore, the convergence of state-owned land and privately-managed telecommunications infrastructure creates complex power dynamics between governments and technology conglomerates (Bratton, 2015). This infrastructural hegemony implies that whoever owns the cables and cell towers effectively governs the flow of global information, shaping public discourse and intellectual property distribution (DeNardis, 2014). The plethora of use cases for this data ranges from targeted consumer advertising to advanced predictive policing, library cataloging analyses, and social engineering architectures (Kitchin, 2021). Therefore, recognizing the foundational ownership of these digital and physical layers is critical for understanding modern geopolitical, economic, and informational structures (Srnicek, 2017).

### Counter-Arguments
While infrastructural control is highly centralized, the rapid rise of decentralized technologies, such as blockchain and peer-to-peer networks, offers potential avenues for circumventing traditional corporate or state monopolies over data flow (De Filippi & Wright, 2018). Furthermore, stringent data protection regulations like the General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) establish legal frameworks that actively enforce the "right to be forgotten," directly challenging the notion that digital permanence is an unavoidable absolute (Floridi, 2015). Finally, advanced encryption technologies empower individuals to secure their data packets, ensuring that even if the physical infrastructure is owned by a surveillance state, the informational content remains inaccessible and private (Greenwald, 2014).
If the physical and digital infrastructure we rely on is fundamentally owned and monitored by centralized powers, to what extent does true individual autonomy or unmonitored intellectual property exist in the modern age?

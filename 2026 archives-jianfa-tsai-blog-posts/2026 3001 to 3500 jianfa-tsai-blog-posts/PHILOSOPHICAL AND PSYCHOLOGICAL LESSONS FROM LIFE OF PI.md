### Discussion of Key Themes
The 2012 cinematic adaptation of Yann Martel's novel, directed by Ang Lee, functions as a multilayered exploration of human resilience, the subjectivity of truth, and the relationship between man and nature.
**1. The Power of Storytelling and Subjective Truth**The central theme of the film is the "better story." At the conclusion, Pi presents two versions of his survival: one featuring animals and another featuring a grim, violent reality involving humans (Ang Lee, 2012). The film posits that objective truth may be less important than the narrative we choose to believe to find meaning in suffering. In the context of psychological resilience, storytelling acts as a cognitive framework that allows individuals to process trauma without being destroyed by it (Martel, 2001).
**2. Faith as a Survival Tool**Pi’s simultaneous practice of Hinduism, Christianity, and Islam symbolizes a universalist approach to faith. The film suggests that faith is not necessarily about adhering to specific dogmas but about maintaining a sense of wonder and hope when faced with the "silent" vastness of the ocean (Stephens, 2012). This "leap of faith" is portrayed as a practical necessity for survival rather than just a spiritual luxury.
**3. The Duality of the Human Soul**Richard Parker, the Bengal tiger, is widely interpreted as a projection of Pi’s own primal instincts. By "taming" the tiger, Pi is actually taming his own capacity for violence and fear. The movie teaches that we must acknowledge our "inner tiger"—our survival instincts—but we must also ensure they do not consume our humanity (Scholes, 2013).
**4. Resilience Through Routine**A practical lesson from Pi’s ordeal is the importance of discipline. By following the survival manual, keeping a diary, and maintaining a schedule for feeding and training Richard Parker, Pi prevents the mental decay often associated with isolation (Ang Lee, 2012).

### Counter-Arguments
One could argue that the film’s conclusion promotes "willful delusion" rather than genuine psychological healing. Critics of the "better story" philosophy suggest that by choosing a beautiful fiction over a harsh reality, one avoids the necessary process of grieving and confronting the truth of human nature (Stephens, 2012). This perspective argues that true growth comes from accepting the "human story" and its flaws, rather than masking them with religious or animal allegories.

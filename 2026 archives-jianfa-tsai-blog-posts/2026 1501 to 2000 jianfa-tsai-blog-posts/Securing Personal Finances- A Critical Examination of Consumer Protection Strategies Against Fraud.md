# PersonalFinance — How to keep your money secured  
Jianfa Ben Tsai

Check your bank account balance and transactions daily; don’t wait till the end of the month.  

Visit https://moneysmart.gov.au/  

If you are hacked, do a credit freeze on your bank accounts and everything in your life.  

Debit or credit card tap-to-pay for products/services has an extra surcharge. Pay by cash gives customers discounts. Does this help to circulate counterfeit cash?  

Change the bank and PayPal website login password after each purchase.  

Don’t access your bank apps or enter your bank app password. At the same time, on the bus, tram, or train, people around you or sitting behind you can learn about your password via peeping over your shoulder or via a secret app video of you entering your passwords. Delete banking apps from your phone and use only one device for online banking, stored in a home safe.  

Avoid sharing credit or debit card numbers for online payments; use PayPal, Samsung Pay, and Apple Pay if you have to.  

It’s essential to have some cash in a secret safe. Criminals could empty your bank accounts.  

Only one bank app on one device for your banking. Nothing more. Eliminate or reduce online banking if possible.  

Keep all your digital and physical purchase receipts, and just match the number of receipts you have with your credit or debit card bank statements.  

When using a bank credit/debit card via phone, turn off NFC when not paying.  

Use an RFID credit or debit card sleeve or an RFID wallet to prevent people from scanning your card from behind you. Keep your card at home safe and use the trusted phone bank app to pay.  

Keep your credit or debit cards locked in the bank app at all times, unlock when you need to pay, then lock your card again.  

Choose a big bank that you can use via the bank phone app, reduce the daily withdrawal amount to $100, toggle lock / unlock international card payment, lock / unlock local EFTPOS payments, and lock/unlock online payments.  

Avoid performing online financial transactions on public or shared devices, or on devices you suspect are compromised.  

Avoid using the library or public computers; hackers often plant viruses in those computers to mine your personal details when you log into your email accounts or plugged in your USB drive which you plug into your home computer to infect your family member computers via home WiFi. Your and your family members’ bank accounts were emptied, and your life cloud data was deleted.  

Never pick up phone calls or reply to SMS from marketers or anyone you don’t know in real life. If the person leaves a voice mail, don’t call back the phone number in the voice mail.  

Google the business phone number and call the business from the website search result. Beforehand, Google “business XYZ review” to determine whether it is a scam.  

Verify any odd or suspicious requests through the official bank’s contact numbers or channels.  

Use a secret dedicated phone number for banking only. Use a dedicated secret phone number for 1st-level blood family only.  

Read the transaction details in the SMS or email alerts carefully. Validate that the messages, for example, check that the account number is correct, or the transaction reflects your request. Do not provide a One-Time Password (OTP) or bank’s digital token authorization if the details in the SMS or email alerts do not match the transaction you initiated. Inform the bank immediately if in doubt.  

Check your transaction history regularly for any abnormal transactions and call the bank immediately.  

Keep your contact details updated with the bank.  

Commit your bank’s username and password to memory; do not write it down on paper, let alone in a password manager or on any computer or phone.  

Choose a random PIN for your ATM card and/or phone identification. Get an ATM card that always requires the payer to enter the PIN code for purchases, regardless of the purchase amount. Never log in to your bank account on public Wi-Fi. Log in to online banking only using the bank’s web browser or the official app. Never any third-party app.  

Sign up for official bank app alerts from your bank whenever a withdrawal is made or a new payee is added to your banking. Enter the URL of your bank every time you visit the bank on the web. Never click links to it. Configure your web browser to remember the wrong password for your online bank login, while only you remember the correct password in your head. Sandbox your bank accounts by opening a bank account with most of your money with Bank A, which doesn’t have online banking, and a bank account with Bank B that has transaction accounts in online banking. Never type in your passport, driver’s license, or other government-issued identity document online, or take a photo of your driver’s license and store it online. If you need to open bank accounts, go to the bank in person.  

Why don’t banking apps after the website/app sign-in page show the last login date & time to max security against unauthorized access/use?  

Count the cash in the wallet every day after you reach home, and count the cash in the wallet again after you wake up.  

Don’t leave money lying around in the jacket or table at home, workplace, or outside because anyone could steal money from you — even your family members.

### Problem Statement
Consumer fraud and identity theft continue to escalate in Australia, with billions lost annually despite regulatory efforts (Neumann, 2025). Tsai's (2020) guidelines address gaps in individual-level protections, yet some recommendations conflict with contemporary best practices endorsed by peer-reviewed research and government frameworks (Smith, 2008; Xu et al., 2022).

### Facts
Daily account monitoring reduces undetected fraud (Smith, 2008). Credit report bans temporarily restrict access to prevent unauthorized applications (Experian, 2026). RFID skimming remains a documented, albeit low-frequency, risk for contactless cards (Button, 2024). Public device use heightens malware exposure (Ho, 2026). Banks provide in-app card controls for toggling payment types (Lucas internal analysis, 2026).

### Supportive Reasoning
Daily balance checks and receipt matching enable early detection, reducing losses by up to 50% in some studies (Smith, 2008). Card locks and RFID sleeves provide tangible barriers against skimming (Button, 2024). Avoiding unknown calls prevents 80% of social engineering (Ho, 2026). Cash reserves offer offline resilience (Tsai, 2020).

### Counter-Arguments
Frequent password changes weaken security through reuse (NIST, 2025). Deleting apps hinders real-time alerts and monitoring (Laxman, 2024). Memorization-only policies increase cognitive load and errors versus managers. Credit "freeze" terminology misleads; Australia uses temporary bans, not full account locks (Experian, 2026). Over-reliance on cash ignores counterfeit and theft risks.

### Conclusion
Tsai's (2020) framework offers valuable foundational vigilance, yet requires modernization to align with evidence-based practices and regulations. Balanced implementation enhances consumer protection while acknowledging convenience trade-offs (Smith, 2008; Neumann, 2025).

### Action Steps
1. Review and update all bank contact details immediately to ensure alerts reach the correct channels (Moneysmart.gov.au framework).
2. Place a temporary credit report ban with Equifax, Experian, and any remaining CRBs if compromise is suspected, following official online processes.
3. Enable in-app card locking features and transaction alerts for all accounts via official bank applications.
4. Install and configure a reputable password manager on a secure primary device, generating unique credentials per NIST guidelines.
5. Conduct daily wallet and account reconciliations, matching receipts to statements each evening and morning.
6. Verify all suspicious communications by calling institutions directly from official websites, never via provided links or numbers.
7. Use only dedicated, secure devices for banking and store physical cards in RFID-protected sleeves when not in use.
8. Attend free Scamwatch webinars or Moneysmart resources quarterly to stay informed on evolving threats.
9. Sandbox major savings in non-online accounts while maintaining monitored transaction accounts with controls enabled.
10. Test RFID sleeves and NFC toggling on a low-value transaction to confirm functionality before full reliance.

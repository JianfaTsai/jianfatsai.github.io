### Problem Statement
In an era of rapid technological disruption and neoliberal educational reforms prioritizing vocational outcomes, the erosion of liberal education risks diminishing graduates' capacity for critical inquiry, ethical decision-making, and adaptability, potentially fostering narrow specialization at the expense of holistic personal and societal development (Zajda, 2014).

### Facts
Liberal education originates from the Latin *artes liberales*, denoting studies suitable for free citizens rather than slaves or tradespeople. It traditionally encompasses the trivium and quadrivium. Contemporary definitions emphasize broad interdisciplinary knowledge plus in-depth study in one area, developing skills such as critical thinking and communication (Association of American Colleges and Universities, as synthesized in Chen, 2021). In Australia, higher education frameworks require graduate attributes aligned with these outcomes, though market-driven policies often favor employability metrics.

### Evidence
Peer-reviewed sources confirm that liberal education correlates with enhanced problem-solving and lifelong learning outcomes (McGovern, 1991). Historical evidence from ancient Athens positions it as foundational to democracy, contrasting with servile training (Montás, 2022). Australian studies highlight tensions between neoliberal policies and broad education, noting impacts on equity and quality (Hong, 2022).

### History
The concept traces to ancient Greece, where Aristotle (ca. 350 BCE/1984) distinguished liberal pursuits (for personal flourishing) from illiberal ones (for utility). Roman thinkers like Cicero and Seneca expanded it, leading to the medieval seven liberal arts. In the 19th century, John Henry Newman (1852/1996) advocated it in *The Idea of a University* as cultivating a "habit of mind." U.S. developments under Charles Eliot introduced electives, while Australian higher education evolved post-1990s globalization toward market liberalization (Zajda, 2014). Temporal context reveals shifts from elite civic preparation to mass-access models amid industrialization.

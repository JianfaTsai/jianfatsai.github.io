**Lightning connector (Lightning)** is Apple's proprietary reversible cable interface used for charging and data on older devices like the Magic Mouse 2.    
**Battery management system (BMS)** is the internal circuitry that controls safe charging, prevents overcharge, and reports percentage levels.  
  
The process is straightforward: Flip the mouse, insert the Lightning end of the included woven USB-C to Lightning cable into the bottom port, and connect the other end to a Mac USB-C port or a compatible USB power adapter. The mouse shows no external charging light, so check progress via macOS: Click the Control Center or Bluetooth menu in the menu bar to view the percentage. Apple recommends leaving the mouse powered on for fastest charging. A completely depleted battery typically reaches full capacity in about two hours under normal conditions.  
  
**Simple explanation:** Think of it like filling a small phone battery — it doesn't take all day because the capacity is modest compared to a laptop.  
  
### Deeper Context, Nuances, and Implications  
- **Official Apple stance:** Apple does not publish an exact "0-100%" time in tech specs, focusing instead on "about a month or more between charges" and noting the included cable. Real-world consensus across Apple Discussions, reviews, and user tests settles at approximately 2 hours for a full charge from zero.  
- **Quick-charge capability:** Even from near-zero, a 2–3 minute plug-in often provides enough power for a full workday (8+ hours), which is a deliberate design trade-off for the slim form factor.  
- **Edge cases and considerations:**  
  - **Slower charging:** Low-power USB ports (e.g., older iMac USB 3), dirty ports, or using the mouse while attempting to charge (impossible anyway) can extend time toward 3+ hours or cause incomplete charges.  
  - **Very depleted battery:** If left dead for weeks/months, it may need longer initial recovery or show no immediate response — overnight charging usually revives it.  
  - **Cable/adapter quality:** Non-Apple or damaged cables can slow or prevent charging; a 5W or higher adapter works, but higher wattage doesn't significantly speed it up due to the mouse's internal limits (typically draws ~0.5–1.5A).  
  - **Cannot use while charging:** The bottom port means the mouse is flipped and unusable, unlike the Magic Keyboard or Trackpad.  
  - **Battery longevity:** Repeated full discharges or extreme temperatures can degrade capacity over years; keeping levels between 20–80% when possible helps, though most users top up monthly without issue.  
  - **Newer USB-C Magic Mouse:** Recent models (USB-C version) follow the same ~2-hour pattern with updated cable.  
  
Thorough testing shows variability is usually under 30–60 minutes depending on setup, but 2 hours is the reliable average for planning.  
  
### Wise humans:  
Wise humans note that the charging "inconvenience" is minor given the device's multi-week battery life — perspective matters: two hours every few weeks is negligible compared to daily phone charging. They emphasize mindful habits, like charging overnight when low, to maintain workflow without frustration. Some compare it to other minimalist Apple designs that prioritize aesthetics and daily experience over perfect convenience in rare moments.  
  
**Counter-arguments:** Critics argue the bottom port is a poor design choice because it forces downtime and prevents use during charging (unlike competitors with side ports or swappable batteries). Heavy users or those in shared workspaces may find the 2-hour full charge plus inability to use the mouse annoying if they forget to monitor battery levels. Slow charging on non-optimal ports can also frustrate travelers.

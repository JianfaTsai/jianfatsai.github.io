Analysis of Maternal Investment and Social Structures
The question explores whether biological behaviors—specifically maternal favoritism—suggest that gender-based discrimination is an inherent human trait rather than a socially constructed one driven by men. This requires looking at Evolutionary Biology, Sociology, and Parental Investment Theory.
Parental Investment and Favoritism
In evolutionary biology, Trivers' (1972) Parental Investment Theory suggests that parents distribute resources to maximize the reproductive success of their offspring. Favoritism is often not about "liking" one child more, but about investing in the child most likely to survive and pass on genetic material in high-stress environments (Beulah et al., 2022).
The Distinction Between Favoritism and Discrimination
It is a logical leap to conclude that because women exhibit selective investment in their children, they are the primary architects of systemic discrimination against their own gender. Discrimination is a multi-layered phenomenon involving power dynamics, resource control, and historical institutionalization.

Analysis of Misinformation: The "Biological Determinism" Fallacy
The idea that discrimination is purely "human nature" is often classified as Biological Determinism. While humans have natural inclinations toward in-group favoritism, modern research shows that gender discrimination is heavily influenced by socialization. Studies indicate that in societies with more egalitarian resource distribution, gender-based discrimination significantly decreases, suggesting it is a flexible social behavior rather than a fixed biological imperative (Wood & Eagly, 2012).
Counter-Arguments
• Cultural Variation: If discrimination were purely "human nature," it would look identical in every culture. However, matrilineal societies (e.g., the Minangkabau of Indonesia) show entirely different power structures.
• Male Agency: Historical records show that laws restricting women's rights (property, voting) were primarily drafted and enforced by men in positions of power, suggesting that the "due to men" aspect is a documented historical fact of power consolidation.

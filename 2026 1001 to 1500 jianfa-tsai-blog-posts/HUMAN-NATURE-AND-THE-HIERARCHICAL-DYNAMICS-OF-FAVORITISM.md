### Abstract Summary

While maternal favoritism suggests a biological and psychological predisposition toward selective resource allocation, it does not necessarily prove that subjugating others is an unchangeable law of human nature; rather, it indicates that humans are evolved to categorize and prioritize based on perceived utility, survival fitness, and social alignment.

### Understanding Favoritism and Subordination (ELI5)

Imagine you have a box of limited snacks; you might give the biggest piece to the friend who helps you the most or the one you think needs it most to stay strong, not because you hate the others, but because your brain is trying to make sure the "team" survives using the best strategy it knows.

### Evolutionary and Psychological Foundations of Bias

The phenomenon of maternal favoritism—often termed "differential parenting"—is well-documented in evolutionary biology. According to the **Trivers-Longman hypothesis**, parents may biasedly invest resources in children who provide the highest reproductive return or survival probability (Suitor et al., 2017). This suggests that human nature is not necessarily "malicious" in its subordination of others, but rather **calculative**.

### The Mechanics of Subordination

Subordination occurs when these internal "preferences" are scaled up to a societal level. Humans use **heuristics** (mental shortcuts) to organize complex social worlds. When these shortcuts involve power dynamics, they manifest as:

1. **Social Identity Theory:** People naturally favor their "in-group" and subordinate the "out-group" to bolster self-esteem and group cohesion (Tajfel & Turner, 1979).
    
2. **Resource Scarcity:** When resources (food, status, money) are perceived as finite, hierarchy becomes a tool for distribution.
    
3. **Gendered Power Structures:** The subordination of females or specific male subgroups often stems from historical labor divisions and the control of reproductive capital (Lerner, 1986).

### Counter-Arguments: The Case for Egalitarianism

It is a mistake to assume "natural" equals "inevitable."

- **Neuroplasticity and Culture:** Human culture has shown a remarkable ability to override biological impulses. The rise of egalitarian societies and human rights frameworks proves that we can consciously choose to dismantle hierarchies (Pinker, 2011).
    
- **Cooperation as Survival:** Many anthropologists argue that early human survival depended more on **hyper-cooperation** than on strict subordination, suggesting that our "nature" is equally geared toward fairness (Bowles & Gintis, 2011).

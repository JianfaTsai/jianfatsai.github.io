### Problem Statement
Overuse of dishwashing liquid remains a pervasive issue in domestic settings, driven by habitual squirting without load assessment, resulting in excess chemical discharge into wastewater systems and unnecessary financial outlays (Stamminger & Schencking, 2023). This inefficiency exacerbates water scarcity concerns in Australia and contributes to broader environmental degradation, despite readily available evidence-based alternatives that align consumption with actual need.

### Facts
Research demonstrates that precise detergent dosing based on dish volume can achieve up to 30% reduction in liquid usage without compromising cleanliness. Australian households consume significant volumes of water and energy in manual dishwashing, often exceeding machine equivalents when practices ignore load size. Phosphates and surfactants in traditional liquids persist in waterways, affecting aquatic life even after treatment.

### Evidence
Peer-reviewed studies confirm that collecting dishes and applying minimal detergent after water filling yields substantial savings in water (60%), energy (70%), and detergent (30%) compared to item-by-item washing (Fuss et al., 2011). Life-cycle assessments reveal that product-use phase dominates environmental burdens in manual cleaning (Keoleian et al., 2020). Australian Water Efficiency Labelling data underscore that optimized handwashing complements regulated appliance standards.

### History
The concept of measured detergent use traces to early 20th-century home economics, formalized in post-World War II consumer guides amid resource rationing. Edge cases, such as greasy pans, benefit from pre-soaking with minimal liquid rather than excess dosing. Multiple perspectives reveal that while initial habit change requires effort, long-term adherence yields cross-domain benefits in household budgeting and environmental metrics.

### Action Steps
1. Observe the pile of dishes and categorize by size—small (under 10 items), medium (10-20), or large (over 20)—before filling the sink.
2. Fill the sink with warm water to the appropriate level based on categorization, then add detergent gradually until light suds appear.
3. Pre-soak heavily soiled items briefly in a minimal detergent solution to loosen residues without additional dosing.
4. Wash items in order from least to most dirty to maximize the initial soapy water’s effectiveness.
5. Rinse under a low-flow tap only as needed, avoiding continuous running water to conserve resources.
6. Track weekly detergent usage in a simple notebook to refine future estimates and identify patterns.
7. Share the method with family members through demonstration sessions to ensure consistent household application.
8. Review local water utility reports quarterly to align personal habits with regional conservation goals.
9. Experiment with eco-friendly concentrated liquids to further minimize volume requirements while maintaining efficacy.
10. Conduct a monthly audit of drain residue to confirm reduced chemical output and adjust techniques accordingly.

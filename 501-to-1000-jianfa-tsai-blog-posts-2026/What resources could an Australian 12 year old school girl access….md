What resources could an Australian 12 year old school girl access to learn about romance, safety, crimes, wisdom, relationships and personal finance?

**AI Analysis:**    
  
An Australian 12‑year‑old can draw on a mix of age‑appropriate government, school and non‑profit resources covering online and personal safety, respectful relationships and early money skills, ideally guided by a trusted adult who can add context and boundaries.    
  
***  
  
## Online and personal safety (including crimes)  
  
For online and general safety, the eSafety Commissioner has kid‑friendly games, videos and classroom packs that teach how to recognise unsafe situations, cyberbullying, grooming and what to do if something feels wrong.[1][2]  
  
The Daniel Morcombe Foundation’s “That’s Suss: eSafety Challenge” is designed specifically for 8–12‑year‑olds and helps them **recognise** red flags, notice body clues and **report** grooming or suspicious behaviour in an interactive story format.[3]  
  
The National Office for Child Safety explains online risks such as grooming, sextortion and unsafe contact and links directly to eSafety’s practical tips on consent, sharing, cyberbullying and how to get help.[4]  
  
State education and child‑safety sites (for example Tasmania’s and others) give families concrete rules for kids aged 5–12, like using supervised devices, thinking carefully about social media readiness and setting limits on what can be shared online.[5]  
  
AFP‑led ThinkUKnow provides resources for children and young people on preventing online child sexual exploitation, and is widely used in Australian schools and by police educators.[6]  
  
***  
  
## Romance, respect, relationships and “wisdom”  
  
Australian “respectful relationships” programs are built into the school curriculum from primary through secondary, teaching students about healthy, safe and respectful relationships, equality and how to recognise controlling or violent behaviour.[7][8]  
  
The Victorian Respectful Relationships initiative explicitly aims to help children build healthy relationships, resilience and confidence and is delivered in government and Catholic schools as a core part of the curriculum from Foundation to Year 12.[8]  
  
Raising Children Network (the government‑endorsed parenting site) has articles on respectful and disrespectful relationships for pre‑teens, with sample questions parents can use (“How do you want to be treated?”) and guidance on conflict, emotions and standing up for oneself, which a 12‑year‑old can also read with an adult.[2][9]  
  
Kids Helpline (for 5–25 year olds) offers confidential phone and online chat support for “all issues”, including crushes, friendships, family conflict and abuse, giving kids a way to ask about romance, relationships and tough situations safely.[2]  
  
These relationship and wellbeing resources are a practical way for a young person to start learning “wisdom” about boundaries, respect, emotional regulation and when to seek help.[9][7][8]  
  
***  
  
## Personal finance and money skills  
  
ASIC’s Moneysmart “Teaching kids about money” gives simple, concrete ideas for showing children where money comes from, budgeting, spending wisely and setting savings goals, targeted at parents but understandable for older children as well.[10]  
  
Talk Money (Ecstra Foundation) provides free, curriculum‑aligned money workshops and resources for Years 5–10, so a 12‑year‑old in upper primary or early secondary may access these sessions and follow‑up materials through her school.[11]  
  
MoneyTime is an online financial literacy course specifically designed for Australian kids aged 10–14, covering earning, saving, budgeting, investing, career choices, peer pressure and staying safe when shopping online, with optional parent‑child modules to embed learning at home.[12][13]  
  
These structured programs give age‑appropriate foundations in budgeting, avoiding “bad debt”, planning for goals and understanding basic consumer risks, which are key elements of personal finance “wisdom” for a 12‑year‑old.[13][10][12]  
  
***  
  
## How an adult might help her use these  
  
A parent, carer or teacher can:    
- Co‑view interactive safety resources (eSafety, That’s Suss, ThinkUKnow) and talk through examples of unsafe behaviour and safe responses.[1][3][4][6]  
- Ask open questions suggested by Raising Children Network to help her think critically about what respectful romantic or friendship behaviour looks like.[9]  
- Enrol or support her in school‑based programs like Respectful Relationships and Talk Money, and, if appropriate, a structured online course such as MoneyTime.[8][11][13]  
  
This shared use of resources helps ensure that learning about romance, safety, crime risks, relationships and money stays accurate, values‑aligned and developmentally appropriate.[4][7][10][2]

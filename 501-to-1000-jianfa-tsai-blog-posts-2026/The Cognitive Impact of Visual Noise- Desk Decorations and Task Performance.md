The problem with having shiny glitter decorations on your desk, near your computer monitor or laptop screen, is that the decorations’ shine competes for your physical and cognitive attention and visually distracts you from reading the text on the screen. Reading the text generates future money, but looking at the shiny decoration on the desk doesn't. Your attention just keeps subliminally, maybe unconsciously, unbeknownst to you, moving to and fro between the screen and the decoration.
### ELI5 (Explain Like I'm 5)
Having bright, shiny things next to your computer is like having someone poke your shoulder while you are trying to read a book; your brain keeps looking at the sparkle instead of the work that helps you grow up and be successful.
### Understanding Visual Interference and Attentional Capture
The presence of high-contrast or reflective objects, such as glitter decorations, within the peripheral vision creates a phenomenon known as **attentional capture**. In cognitive psychology, this refers to the process where a stimulus involuntarily diverts attention away from a primary task (Theeuwes, 2010). Because the human eye is biologically programmed to detect motion and light changes—a survival mechanism—the "sparkle" of glitter acts as a constant, low-level signal of change, even if the object is stationary.
### The Mechanism of Cognitive Load and Task Switching
When your gaze shifts from the monitor to a decoration, you are engaging in **micro-task switching**. While it may only take milliseconds, the cumulative "switching cost" reduces the efficiency of the Prefrontal Cortex, which is responsible for deep work and executive function.

### Multidimensional Analysis of Peripheral Distraction
- **The Saccadic Eye Movement:** Even if you do not consciously "look" at the decoration, your eyes perform microsaccades. If a high-luminance object is in the periphery, the brain must actively suppress the urge to center that object in the fovea (the sharpest part of the eye) (American Psychological Association [APA], 2022).
- **Environmental Psychology:** Workspace ergonomics often focus on physical comfort, but "cognitive ergonomics" suggests that a minimalist visual field is superior for prolonged concentration (Knight & Haslam, 2010).
- **The "Future Money" Variable:** Distraction directly impacts the **Opportunity Cost**. Every minute spent recalibrating focus after a visual distraction is a minute not spent on high-value output.
### Counter-Argument: The "Enriched Environment" Hypothesis
Some research suggests that a completely sterile environment can lead to boredom and reduced creativity. The "Enriched Office" theory posits that personal items can improve well-being and job satisfaction, potentially leading to higher long-term productivity through emotional regulation (Knight & Haslam, 2010). However, this usually applies to non-reflective items like plants rather than high-stimulus items like glitter.

### Action Steps for Improvement
1. **Conduct a 30-Degree Sweep:** Clear all high-contrast, reflective, or moving objects from within a 30-degree radius of your monitor to protect your primary visual field.
2. **Apply the "Productivity Tax" Logic:** Before placing an item on your desk, ask: "Does this item assist in the generation of the work on my screen?" If the answer is no, move it to a shelf behind you.
3. **Implement Visual "Deep Work" Zones:** Use a desk mat of a neutral, matte color to reduce reflections from desk lamps or overhead lighting.

### Literature Review
Peer-reviewed research consistently links lifelong learning mindsets to improved career outcomes. For instance, empirical studies in accounting and finance demonstrate that professionals with stronger lifelong learning orientations report higher career success metrics, such as promotions and job satisfaction (Ates & Kadir, 2012; Mocker & Spear, 1982). Similarly, analyses of lifetime earnings reveal substantial gaps based on educational fields and continuous upskilling, with college graduates in certain disciplines earning up to 50% more over 40-year careers than high school counterparts (Kim et al., 2015). In Australia, investigations into continuous professional development (CPD) affirm its role in boosting employability and competitiveness (Windsor Group, n.d.). However, historiographical evolution in career transition literature shows shifting perspectives from linear career stages to dynamic, relational models, underscoring the need for adaptive documentation like resumes (Sullivan & Al Ariss, 2021). Source criticism reveals potential biases in self-reported data, where intent to promote education may inflate perceived benefits, yet temporal context from 2015–2025 studies confirms relevance amid post-pandemic labor shifts.

### Supportive Reasoning
Updating qualifications and resumes demonstrably correlates with enhanced employability and earnings. A lifelong learning mindset increases career success by one unit per incremental attitude improvement among professionals (ResearchGate publication, 2021). In wealth accumulation terms, work trajectories tied to continuous development yield higher lifetime rewards, as seen in U.S. data where field-specific education premiums persist over decades (Gruijters, 2023; Kim et al., 2015). Australian contexts reinforce this, with CPD fostering skills that align with industry demands, leading to promotions and reduced unemployment risks (Adecco, 2023). Real-world scalability for individuals involves low-cost online certifications, amplifying long-term financial stability without organizational affiliation.

### Counter-Arguments
Despite benefits, not all updates guarantee proportional gains. Correlation does not imply causation; external factors like economic downturns or market saturation may nullify resume enhancements (Sullivan & Al Ariss, 2021). Time and financial investments in qualifications can strain resources, particularly for early-career individuals, potentially delaying wealth building (Aspen Institute, 2018). In some sectors, over-qualification leads to underemployment, where updated resumes signal mismatch rather than value (Kim et al., 2015). Devil’s advocate: rapid technological obsolescence renders frequent updates inefficient, and innate talent or networking often outweighs documented credentials in success narratives.

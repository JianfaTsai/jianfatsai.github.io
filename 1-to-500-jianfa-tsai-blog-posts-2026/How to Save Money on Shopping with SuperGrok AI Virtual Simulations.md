# How to Save Money on Shopping with SuperGrok AI Virtual Simulations

Ask Grok AI app to: 
1. Generate an AI photo of Product X in different material forms or types. 
2. Search via Google to locate royalty-free or purchase a professionally designed image of Product X.
3. Ask an AI to describe the metadata and manufacturing details of Product X. Then use the aforementioned information as a base reference when uploading to Grok’s Imagine feature, together with your iPad Pro Freeform app and Apple Pencil hand-drawn image of your ideal Product Y. 
4. In the Grok app’s Imagine feature, craft your prompt to use the material, texture, colour, and other metadata (dimensions in millimetres, for example, height, width, and depth, or communicated in a hand-drawn image on iPad) of Product X to recreate Product Y as a realistic video or photo. 
5. Use the output and your selfie photo to create a second video of yourself playing with or using the product, ending the scene by zooming in on Product Y.
6. Alternatively, upload a photo of yourself and a photo of a previously Grok AI-generated image of a “product similar to the item you want to buy online but can’t afford”. Tell Grok AI to create a video of yourself or an avatar of yourself playing or using the item. This way, you don’t need to buy the physical item.

### Relevant Federal, State or Local Laws in Australia:
Personal use of AI generated self simulations for private visualisation generally falls outside regulated activities however public sharing of such content may engage certain provisions.
Under the Criminal Code Amendment (Deepfake Sexual Material) Act 2024 non consensual sharing of intimate deepfakes carries a maximum penalty of six years imprisonment though this does not apply to non sexual product visualisation videos.
In South Australia nation leading deepfake laws effective November 2025 impose fines of up to 20000 dollars or four years imprisonment for creating or distributing AI generated images or videos that are humiliating degrading invasive or sexually explicit even if entirely synthetic.
The Privacy Act 1988 requires entities handling personal information including inferred or generated data from AI such as selfies or body metrics to ensure collection is reasonably necessary and lawful with no specific maximum fine listed but civil penalties up to 2500000 dollars for serious breaches by organisations.
Australian Consumer Law section 18 prohibits misleading or deceptive conduct in trade or commerce with civil penalties up to 50000000 dollars for corporations though personal non commercial videos are unlikely to trigger enforcement.
The Copyright Act 1968 protects against unauthorised reproduction of copyrighted designs such as specific product aesthetics with remedies including injunctions and damages but fair dealing exceptions may apply for private study or research.
Online Safety Act 2021 empowers the eSafety Commissioner to issue removal notices for harmful content with fines up to 782500 dollars for non compliance by platforms though personal AI videos of jewellery use rarely qualify as harmful.
No direct prohibition exists on creating or privately using AI videos of oneself wearing simulated luxury items such as a gold necklace with imperial jade dragon pendant.

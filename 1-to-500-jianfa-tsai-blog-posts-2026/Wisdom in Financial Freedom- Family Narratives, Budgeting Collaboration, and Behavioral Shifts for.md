### Facts
Family financial discussions positively correlate with improved financial literacy and behaviors among younger generations, as evidenced by multiple studies on financial socialization processes (Gudmunson & Danes, 2011; Kelley et al., 2020). In Australia, no gift tax applies to monetary transfers between family members, meaning neither the giver nor recipient owes income tax on such gifts under Australian Taxation Office rules (Australian Taxation Office, n.d.; Services Australia, 2024). However, gifting impacts Centrelink assessments for pensions, where amounts exceeding $10,000 in a financial year or $30,000 over five years are deemed deprived assets for five years (Services Australia, 2024). Intergenerational wealth transmission, including through stories and direct support, influences young adults' financial independence and human capital attainment (Johnson & Benson, 2012, as cited in Addo & Lichter, 2013). The common quote on behavioral inertia traces to Jessie Potter's 1981 presentation, underscoring the need for change to achieve different results (Quote Investigator, 2016). Exploration of family histories can reveal patterns in financial decision-making across generations, though empirical data on "family secrets" specifically remains limited in peer-reviewed literature (Nakagawa & Shigekawa, 2024).

### Problem Statement

In the Australian context, this manifests as potential Centrelink benefit reductions from improper gifting or missed opportunities for superannuation contributions, while cultural taboos around discussing family financial histories limit knowledge transfer (Services Australia, 2024; Sundarasen et al., 2026).

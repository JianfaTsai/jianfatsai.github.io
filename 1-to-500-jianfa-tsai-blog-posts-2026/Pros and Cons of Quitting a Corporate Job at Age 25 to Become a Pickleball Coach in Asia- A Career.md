The Australian Taxation Office (ATO) maintains that individuals who remain tax residents must declare worldwide income, including earnings from coaching activities abroad, even if taxed in the host country (Australian Taxation Office, 2025). State laws in Victoria, where the inquirer resides, do not impose additional restrictions on resignation but align with federal standards for notice and final pay. No local Melbourne ordinances directly govern international career moves, yet tax residency tests consider factors such as duration abroad and ties to Australia. ### Counter-Arguments
Despite potential rewards, significant drawbacks warrant careful consideration. Income from coaching often proves variable and lacks the structured benefits of corporate employment, such as paid leave or health coverage. Visa and work permit processes in Asian countries frequently require employer sponsorship, creating bureaucratic hurdles and dependency risks (VisaEnvoy, n.d.). A resume gap from leaving corporate work may complicate future returns to traditional roles, especially if hiring managers view the move as unstable. Culture shock, isolation from family networks in Australia, and adaptation to different professional norms can strain mental health. Moreover, the sport’s rapid growth does not guarantee sustained demand everywhere, as market saturation or economic shifts could reduce opportunities over time.

### Abstract
Extreme frugality tactics, such as dual-apartment family housing near parks, yard simplification, signage for call cost awareness, nighttime flashlight use, brightness minimization, dedicated drinkware, raw-food meal substitution, refrigerator shutdown for singles, and intermittent meal skipping from young adulthood, promise substantial household expenditure reductions. However, empirical literature reveals nuanced outcomes: intermittent fasting demonstrates metabolic benefits yet carries adherence challenges (Song et al., 2022), while raw diets risk nutritional imbalances (Carmody, 2009). This article applies historiographical scrutiny to source biases, temporal contexts, and evolving scholarly views on frugality. Findings indicate limited long-term viability without customization, with recommendations emphasizing safety, equity, and integration into broader financial literacy frameworks. Implications extend to individual scalability and organizational wellness programs in Australia.

### Abbreviations and Glossary
IF: Intermittent Fasting – Cyclical eating patterns alternating fasting and consumption periods (de Cabo & Mattson, 2019).  
Frugality: Intentional resource conservation prioritizing long-term financial security over immediate consumption (Appuhami, 2024).  
Raw Food Diet: Consumption of uncooked, unprocessed plant-based foods, excluding specified animal products per common sense (Varzakas, 2024).

### Facts
Peer-reviewed studies confirm intermittent fasting can support weight management and metabolic health in controlled settings, though results vary by individual physiology (Wang et al., 2022). Raw food diets reduce cooking energy use but may lower nutrient bioavailability for certain vitamins (Carmody, 2009). Simple behavioral changes, such as device brightness reduction and single-cup usage, yield measurable but incremental electricity and water savings in residential settings.

### Evidence
Narrative reviews of intermittent fasting highlight reductions in insulin resistance and inflammation markers among overweight adults (Sun et al., 2024). Questionnaire-based surveys on long-term raw diets document weight fluctuations and menstrual irregularities in some participants (Carmody, 2009). Frugality literature links intentional simplicity to improved family financial resilience, particularly when led by primary household managers (research on housewives’ roles in frugal practices; see related studies in productivity journals).

### History

Frugal living philosophies evolved from Depression-era necessity to contemporary voluntary simplicity movements, often critiqued for class-based accessibility biases.

Selected tactics offer marginal savings in targeted areas (e.g., yard maintenance elimination, single-cup protocols) but face counter-evidence on health (raw diets) and practicality (refrigerator deactivation leading to spoilage). Dual-apartment strategies may foster family proximity but inflate initial capital outlay.

### Supportive Reasoning
These tactics align with evidence that small behavioral changes compound savings (Appuhami, 2024) and that intermittent fasting supports metabolic health in young adults (Wang et al., 2022), enabling earlier retirement investing. Yard elimination and single-cup use promote minimalism with proven resource reductions. Dual apartments foster family cohesion, a culturally valued Australian practice.

### Counter-Arguments
Raw diets risk bacterial contamination and nutrient loss, contradicting long-term health data (Carmody, 2009). Refrigerator shutdown invites food waste and safety issues, while meal skipping may trigger disordered eating in vulnerable youth. Dual-apartment purchases contradict “saving” by requiring substantial upfront capital, potentially increasing debt. Historiographical devil’s advocate: early enthusiasm for fasting overlooked modern sedentary lifestyles, introducing temporal mismatch.

### Immediate Consequences

### Long-Term Consequences

### Proposed Improvements

### Conclusion
While creatively inspiring, these outlandish tactics require evidence-based refinement to balance savings with well-being. Historiographical scrutiny reveals evolving views on frugality as both virtue and potential excess, urging informed, personalized application in Australian contexts.

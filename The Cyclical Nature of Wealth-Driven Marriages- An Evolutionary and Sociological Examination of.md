### Abstract
This article critically examines the assertion that mate preferences—women prioritizing wealth and men prioritizing youth and attractiveness—create inherently unstable romantic foundations, often culminating in prenuptial-enabled replacement of partners around age 30. Drawing on evolutionary psychology, sociology, and Australian family law, the analysis reveals partial empirical support for these preferences as adaptive strategies while highlighting their malleability amid cultural shifts. Balanced evaluation demonstrates that while patterns persist among high-status individuals, they do not represent a universal 5,000-year global script nor preclude companionate, enduring marriages. Implications for individual well-being, legal protections, and societal equity are discussed, emphasizing agency over determinism.

### Keywords

### Glossary
- **Hypergamy**: The practice of marrying or partnering with someone of higher socioeconomic status, often observed in women seeking resource security.
- **Prenuptial Agreement (Prenup)**: A legally binding contract signed before marriage outlining asset division upon separation or divorce.
- **Binding Financial Agreement (BFA)**: The Australian legal term for enforceable prenups under the Family Law Act 1975.
- **Evolutionary Psychology**: A framework explaining human behaviors, including mate selection, as adaptations shaped by ancestral survival and reproduction pressures.
- **Serial Monogamy**: A pattern of successive exclusive relationships, often involving replacement of partners rather than lifelong monogamy.

Human mate selection has long fascinated scholars across disciplines, reflecting intersections of biology, culture, and economics. Tsai’s (2026) observation captures a perceived transactional dynamic in romance: women gravitate toward resource-rich partners, while affluent men favor youthful, attractive companions. ### Federal, State, or Local Laws in Australia
In Australia, prenuptial agreements fall under the Family Law Act 1975 (Cth) as Binding Financial Agreements (BFAs). Introduced in 2000 via amendments, BFAs under Sections 90B (pre-marriage), 90C (during marriage), or 90D (post-divorce) allow couples to contractually determine property division and spousal maintenance, ousting court discretion if valid. For enforceability, agreements must be written, signed, and accompanied by independent legal advice certificates confirming each party understood the advantages, disadvantages, and rights waived. Courts may set aside BFAs for fraud, duress, undue influence, non-disclosure, material changes causing child hardship, or unconscionable conduct. No federal, state, or local laws mandate prenups, but they are increasingly common among high-net-worth individuals in Victoria and nationally. Enforcement remains discretionary, balancing contractual freedom with equitable outcomes under the Act’s “just and equitable” test. Uncertainties persist in case law regarding future changes in circumstances.

### Results
Peer-reviewed evidence partially corroborates Tsai’s (2026) core preferences. Across 37 cultures, women valued financial prospects more than men, while men prioritized physical attractiveness and relative youth (Buss, 1989). Men consistently prefer partners 2.5–3 years younger, with gaps widening in remarriages among high-status males (Buss & Schmitt, 2019). Hypergamy persists in resource-driven contexts but shows decline in educationally egalitarian societies, where wives’ advantages no longer elevate divorce risk (Esteve et al., 2016). Prenup utilization correlates with wealth asymmetry, enabling asset protection but not guaranteeing marital longevity. Historical records indicate arranged exchanges (bride price/service) in hunter-gatherer and ancient societies, supporting regulated mate trading but not universal “replacement” at age 30. Australian data reveal BFAs protect high earners but face judicial scrutiny for fairness.

### Supportive Reasoning
Evolutionary psychology provides robust support for the described preferences as adaptations. Parental investment theory posits women, bearing higher reproductive costs, select mates offering resources for offspring survival, while men, limited by sperm but not gestation, prioritize fertility cues like youth and attractiveness (Buss, 1989). Cross-cultural replication and behavioral manifestations—such as larger age gaps among wealthy men’s remarriages—affirm these as psychological universals rather than cultural artifacts. Serial replacement cycles mirror ancestral polygynous tendencies among high-status males, enhancing reproductive success. Prenups function as modern analogs to historical bridewealth contracts, safeguarding male investment. This framework explains Tsai’s (2026) 5,000-year “script” as rooted in reproductive asymmetries observable from hunter-gatherer eras onward.

### Counter-Arguments
Social role theory counters that preferences stem from structural power imbalances rather than innate biology; as gender equality rises, differences diminish (Eagly & Wood, 1999, as discussed in Zhang et al., 2018 preprint). Recent global data show declining educational hypergamy and stable divorce risks when wives out-earn husbands, suggesting adaptation to dual-income norms (Esteve et al., 2016; Schwartz & Han, 2014). Many marriages thrive on companionate love, shared values, and communication, overriding initial preferences. Not all wealthy men engage in replacement; individual variation, personality, and cultural norms explain long-term unions. Historical claims overstate uniformity—monogamy and mutual choice have coexisted alongside elite polygyny for millennia. Prenups, while protective, do not predetermine dissolution; judicial safeguards prevent exploitation.

### Discussion
Integrating cross-domain insights, mate preferences interact with modern economics and law to produce nuanced outcomes. While evolutionary pressures explain average tendencies, cultural evolution—evident in shrinking age gaps and female economic empowerment—demonstrates malleability. Edge cases include high-divorce-risk age gaps versus resilient egalitarian pairings. Disinformation risks arise from oversimplifying preferences as destiny, ignoring 70% female-initiated divorces driven by emotional dissatisfaction rather than aging alone. Practical scalability: individuals can leverage awareness for better matching; organizations (e.g., counseling services) promote premarital education. Uncertainties include self-report biases in preference studies and underrepresentation of non-Western, non-heterosexual contexts. Overall, Tsai’s (2026) critique highlights real vulnerabilities without negating human capacity for enduring bonds.

### Real-Life Examples
Among high-net-worth cohorts, patterns align partially with the claim: studies of elite males show remarriages to partners decades younger, mirroring resource-for-youth trades. Conversely, countless middle-class Australian couples maintain stable marriages through mutual growth, defying replacement narratives. Historical examples from ancient Mesopotamia reveal bride auctions balancing beauty with dowries, yet modern dual-career families prioritize compatibility over initial status or looks.

### Wise Perspectives
Psychologist David Buss emphasizes preferences as flexible strategies, not rigid scripts: “Mate value is multifaceted” (Buss & Schmitt, 2019). Historians note marriage’s evolution from economic alliance to emotional partnership, urging critical evaluation of power dynamics. Relationship experts advocate viewing partnerships as collaborative investments, fostering resilience beyond superficial traits.

### Conclusion
Tsai’s (2026) assertion illuminates persistent mate selection patterns rooted in evolutionary history, yet overgeneralizes their inevitability. While wealth-youth exchanges and prenup-enabled cycles occur, evidence supports both their partial validity and their transcendence through cultural progress and individual agency. Balanced analysis affirms the need for realistic expectations without endorsing transactional dehumanization.

### Problem Statement
State Library Victoria’s publicly accessible research guide contains an embedded hyperlink to a now-defunct Yellow Pages directory search, creating a dead end for users seeking original newspapers. This issue persists despite the guide’s February 2026 update, potentially frustrating library patrons and undermining the institution’s role as a reliable information provider (Glasser, 2012).

### Facts
The exact paragraph on the guide reads: “If you would like to buy an original newspaper, you can ask second-hand or antiquarian bookstores or contact PaperWorld which sells original newspapers,” with the first phrase hyperlinked to a Yellow Pages URL that currently displays “No results found” (State Library Victoria, 2026). PaperWorld’s site remains operational and authenticates original newspapers from Australian archives. Verified Melbourne antiquarian booksellers, including Kay Craddock Antiquarian Bookseller at 156 Collins Street, stock rare materials but are not listed in the outdated directory.

### Evidence
Direct page inspection confirms the hyperlink remains active and points to the non-productive Yellow Pages result (State Library Victoria, 2026). Peer-reviewed studies document link rot as a pervasive issue: approximately 70% of news-related links disappear within a day, and academic citations suffer similar decay (UCSB Library, 2022). Library-specific research shows broken external links erode user confidence and increase support ticket volume (Glasser, 2012).

### Findings
The hyperlink delivers zero value and may deter users from pursuing legitimate purchases. PaperWorld remains a viable alternative. Multiple Melbourne-based antiquarian sellers exist but require manual discovery. Regular link audits are standard best practice yet evidently under-implemented here.

### Analysis
Supportive reasoning: Updating the link or rephrasing prevents wasted clicks, aligns with public service mandates, and models digital stewardship (Glasser, 2012). Counter-arguments: Maintenance burdens small IT teams; removing links entirely could reduce discoverability of legitimate secondary markets. Edge cases include international users versus local Melbourne visitors; nuances arise if the library prioritizes its own copying service over commercial sales. Real-world example: University of California libraries proactively adopt link-rot solutions to protect scholarly citations (UCSB Library, 2022). Implications include eroded public trust if unaddressed, yet scalable fixes (automated checkers) offer low-cost remediation.

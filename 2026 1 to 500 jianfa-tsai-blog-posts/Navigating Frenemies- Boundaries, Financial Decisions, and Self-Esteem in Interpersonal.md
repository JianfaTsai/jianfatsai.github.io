### Facts
Empirical research confirms that toxic or ambivalent friendships correlate with elevated stress, inflammation, and diminished psychological well-being (Pezirkianidis et al., 2023). Friendship victimization, including relational aggression and control tactics common among frenemies, links to increased depressive symptoms in adolescents and extends patterns into adulthood (Dryburgh et al., 2024). Social comparison theory explains why some individuals derive satisfaction from peers' misfortunes, particularly in financial domains, as it bolsters relative self-esteem (Rahimah et al., 2022). In Australia, personal loans between friends or family constitute binding verbal contracts under common law, though enforcement proves challenging without documentation (Slater & Gordon, 2015). Self-esteem issues often exacerbate vulnerability to manipulative relationships, as low self-worth impairs boundary-setting (Marter, 2025).

### Problem Statement
The user's input highlights a pervasive challenge: frenemies exploit emotional and financial vulnerabilities, undermining personal frugality, self-esteem, and resource security while providing no reciprocal support. This dynamic fosters dependency, guilt, and isolation, particularly when individuals lack scrutiny in admitting others into their lives. Without clear boundaries, such relationships erode mental health and financial stability, yet societal pressures to maintain social ties complicate discernment and elimination of harmful influences (Felmlee & Faris, 2016).

### Literature Review
Scholarly inquiry into toxic friendships reveals consistent patterns of victimization and ambivalence. Dryburgh et al. (2024) developed the Friendship Victimization Scale for Adolescents (FVS-A), identifying relational aggression, physical/verbal harm, and controlling behaviors as prevalent, with strong associations to depressive symptoms. Pezirkianidis et al. (2023) conducted a systematic review of 38 studies, confirming that high-quality friendships enhance well-being, whereas toxic ones correlate with psychological distress. Rahimah et al. (2022) specifically linked toxic friendships to diminished self-esteem and well-being among university students. Ambivalent relationships, termed frenemies, exacerbate stress via unpredictability, as noted by Felmlee and Faris (2016) in their analysis of toxic ties in social networks. Financial dimensions remain underexplored academically but appear in applied psychology, where weak boundaries lead to resentment and anxiety (Marter, 2025). Australian sources emphasize documentation for personal loans to mitigate disputes (Armstrong Legal, n.d.). Historiographically, early friendship scholarship idealized elective ties (Roseneil, 2004), but recent critical inquiry accounts for ambivalences and power imbalances.

### Supportive Reasoning
Evidence robustly supports the user's advocacy for boundary enforcement and frenemy elimination. Toxic relationships demonstrably impair mental health via chronic stress and lowered self-worth, validating professional help-seeking (Pezirkianidis et al., 2023). Financial scrutiny prevents resource drainage, aligning with social exchange theory: equitable reciprocity sustains healthy ties (Rahimah et al., 2022). Discarding non-beneficial influences fosters self-respect and aligns with evolutionary psychology, where selective affiliation conserves energy for supportive bonds. In practice, this yields scalable benefits, such as improved budgeting autonomy and reduced manipulation susceptibility.

### Counter-Arguments
Conversely, indiscriminate discarding risks social isolation and overlooks redeemable relationships. Not all financial non-reciprocity signals toxicity; economic disparities or temporary hardships may explain reluctance to lend without malice (Marter, 2025). Ambivalent friendships can provide intermittent support or growth through conflict resolution, per Rawlins (1992) on dialectical tensions. Overemphasizing scrutiny may stem from paranoia or low self-esteem, potentially alienating genuine allies. Cultural norms in collectivist contexts value endurance over elimination, challenging universal applicability (Lahad, 2023). Thus, nuanced discernment, rather than outright rejection, merits consideration to avoid echo chambers.

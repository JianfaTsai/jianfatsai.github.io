### Federal, State, or Local Laws in Australia
Australian federal law under the Australian Consumer Law (Competition and Consumer Act 2010) mandates fair trading practices and provides remedies for misleading conduct or defective services, including online purchases, but imposes no direct prohibition on storing card details (Australian Competition and Consumer Commission [ACCC], 2026). Instead, it empowers consumers to seek refunds or chargebacks for undelivered goods or fraudulent transactions. Visa and Mastercard’s Zero Liability policies, applicable to Australian-issued cards, shield consumers from liability for unauthorized use, shifting the burden to issuing banks unless negligence is proven (Visa, 2026). State-level guidance, such as from Consumer Affairs Victoria and Western Australia’s ScamNet, reinforces best practices like using credit cards over debit for enhanced dispute rights and avoiding direct bank transfers (Consumer Protection Western Australia, 2025). No local Victorian statutes specifically address gift card usage in e-commerce; however, prepaid card regulations fall under federal financial services oversight to prevent fraud. Historiographically, these frameworks evolved from 1990s electronic funds transfer codes to address “card-not-present” fraud post-2000s digitization.

### Results
Analysis reveals that avoiding stored card details reduces breach exposure, as evidenced by reports linking data retention to higher fraud incidence (Forbes, 2019, updated context 2026). Gift card adoption limits losses to prepaid amounts, aligning with risk-minimization principles. However, results also highlight limitations: gift cards function similarly to cash, offering no built-in dispute resolution, and Australian advisories prioritize credit cards or digital wallets for superior protections (Cyber.gov.au, 2026). Supportive data from payment security studies confirm consumer hesitancy toward stored details, while counter-evidence points to tokenized systems rendering storage safer than in prior decades.

### Supportive Reasoning
Tsai’s (2026) strategy effectively deters hacking by eliminating persistent data repositories on third-party servers, a tactic supported by research demonstrating that stored credentials amplify breach impacts (Experian, 2020). Gift cards cap financial risk at the loaded value, preventing cascading unauthorized withdrawals—a critical safeguard for debit accounts directly linked to checking balances. In Australia, this complements Cyber.gov.au (2026) guidance favoring limited-exposure methods. Cross-domain insights from behavioral economics reveal reduced impulse spending and heightened vigilance, fostering long-term security habits. Evidence provenance traces to post-2010s breach analyses, confirming the advice’s evolution from early internet warnings.

### Counter-Arguments
Critics note that modern encryption and tokenization have diminished risks of stored details, with many platforms employing PCI-DSS compliance to render data useless even if breached (Houcheimi et al., 2024). Credit cards, unlike gift cards, provide zero-liability protections and easy chargebacks under Australian law, making them preferable for disputes over faulty goods (ACCC, 2026). Gift cards introduce inconveniences, such as non-refundable balances or scam vulnerabilities where tampered cards drain funds pre-purchase. Historiographical evaluation reveals a shift: early 2000s advice mirrored Tsai’s due to rudimentary security, but contemporary frameworks favor layered defenses like 2FA and virtual cards, potentially rendering strict non-storage overly conservative. Debit cards, per Tsai, carry higher direct-loss risks, yet overall banking systems reimburse most fraud cases upon verification.

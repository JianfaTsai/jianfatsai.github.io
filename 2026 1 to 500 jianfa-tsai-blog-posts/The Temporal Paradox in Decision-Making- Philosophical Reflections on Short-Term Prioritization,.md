### Facts
Behavioral economics demonstrates that humans exhibit present bias, also known as hyperbolic discounting, leading them to overvalue immediate rewards while undervaluing future ones (Wong, 2020). Order effects, including primacy and recency biases, consistently shape decisions by making the first or last options more memorable or influential in choice sets (Yoo et al., 2025). Human average lifespan remains under 100 years in most populations, while Earth’s geological history spans approximately 4.5 billion years, with projections of continued existence for billions more (Scheidel, as cited in BBC Future, 2018). Air, though invisible, constitutes a critical public good essential for biological survival, contrasting with tangible assets in economic valuations of intangibles (Saint-Exupéry, 1943, as referenced in philosophical adaptations). Financial buffers mitigate risks from unexpected events, aligning with prudent risk management principles in personal finance literature.

### Problem Statement
The core problem lies in humanity’s systematic tendency to prioritize short-term gains and visible material concerns, often at the expense of long-term well-being, life enjoyment, and appreciation of invisible essentials like health, time, and relationships (Enke, 2023). This bias creates tension between fiscal responsibility (e.g., saving buffers) and existential fulfillment, especially given life’s brevity relative to planetary timescales, potentially leading to regret, unhappiness, or missed opportunities for happiness.

### Abbreviations and Glossary
- **Present Bias**: Tendency to favor immediate rewards over larger future ones (hyperbolic discounting).
- **Primacy/Recency Effects**: Preference for first or last items in a sequence due to memory processes.
- **Memento Mori**: Latin for “remember that you must die,” a philosophical reminder of life’s brevity.
- **Intangibles**: Non-physical elements like air, time, or relationships with high existential value.

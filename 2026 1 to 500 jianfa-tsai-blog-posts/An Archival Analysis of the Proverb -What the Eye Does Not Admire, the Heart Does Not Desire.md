### Facts
The proverb functions as an illustrative quotation in English-as-a-second-language pedagogy, grouped thematically under money-related sayings to demonstrate real-world language patterns (Keddie, n.d.). Multiple independent sources consistently interpret it as highlighting the eyes' role as the gateway to the heart, where unseen objects or experiences fail to spark desire (African Proverbs Page, n.d.; Universal nature of symbolism, 2016). It surfaces in popular media, including dialogue in the television series *Poldark* (Season 2, Episode 5), where a character employs it to explore themes of romantic temptation and visual attraction (Graham, as adapted in BBC production, 2016). Variations exist across cultures, such as the similar English idiom "What the eyes don't see, the heart doesn't grieve over," confirming its alignment with broader folk expressions on perception and emotion (Dundes, 1980, as referenced in proverb studies). No peer-reviewed empirical study directly tests this exact proverb; its validity rests on anecdotal cultural transmission rather than controlled experimentation.

### Problem Statement
The core problem addressed by the proverb lies in the modern challenge of managing desire in a visually saturated world, where constant exposure to images via digital media, advertising, and social platforms can cultivate unwanted longings or impulses (Twenge, 2017). Without critical awareness of what the eyes encounter, individuals risk allowing external stimuli to dictate internal emotional states, potentially leading to dissatisfaction, consumerism, or moral compromise. This issue is compounded by cultural differences in proverb interpretation, where Western individualistic views may prioritize personal agency over the collective folk wisdom embedded in the saying. Edge cases include individuals with visual impairments, for whom non-visual senses shape desire differently, revealing the proverb's potential cultural bias toward sighted experience (World Health Organization, 2023).

### Counter-Arguments
Critics contend the proverb oversimplifies human motivation; desires can arise from non-visual sources such as auditory cues, memory, or abstract concepts, as evidenced by blind individuals' rich emotional lives (World Health Organization, 2023). Devil's advocate: in a globalized economy, shielding the eyes from diversity could limit cultural appreciation and innovation.

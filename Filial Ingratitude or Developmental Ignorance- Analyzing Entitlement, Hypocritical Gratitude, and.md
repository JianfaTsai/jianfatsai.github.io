### Problem Statement
The described scenario highlights a troubling pattern in some parent-adult child relationships: financial dependence coupled with a striking absence of verbal or behavioral gratitude, ironically juxtaposed against performative acts like singing a song of appreciation. This raises fundamental questions about moral character, emotional development, and family obligations in modern societies where economic pressures often extend parental support into adulthood (Candel et al., 2022). Without clear boundaries or expressions of thanks, such dynamics risk eroding trust, fostering resentment, and perpetuating cycles of entitlement.

### Abstract
This article examines whether repeated financial extraction from parents without gratitude—exemplified by using funds for leisure activities like karaoke while singing performative “thank you” songs—stems from malicious intent (“evil heart”) or developmental unawareness (ignorance). Drawing on peer-reviewed psychology literature on entitlement, filial piety, and family dynamics, the analysis balances supportive evidence for ignorance rooted in overparenting with counterarguments highlighting selfishness. Australian legal contexts are reviewed, alongside practical recommendations. Findings suggest the behavior more commonly reflects learned entitlement than inherent evil, yet carries significant relational risks. Implications for family communication and boundary-setting are discussed.

### Introduction
Parent-adult child financial relationships have evolved amid rising living costs and delayed independence, yet expressions of gratitude remain foundational to healthy bonds (Bedford, 2019). The queried behavior—accepting money for non-essential pursuits while failing to verbalize thanks—exemplifies a disconnect that challenges traditional notions of reciprocity. This paper applies critical inquiry, evaluating temporal, cultural, and psychological contexts, to determine underlying motives without oversimplifying human complexity.

### Literature Review
Peer-reviewed studies consistently link lack of gratitude in adult children to overinvolved parenting styles that inadvertently foster psychological entitlement. For instance, Candel et al. (2022) found that helicopter parenting correlates with excessive relational entitlement in emerging adults, mediating poorer relational outcomes. Similarly, Tolmacz et al. (2022) developed the SRE-ao scale, demonstrating that imbalanced entitlement perceptions in families associate with anxiety, depression, and reduced belongingness.  

Filial piety research reveals cultural nuances: traditional models emphasize gratitude-driven care (Bedford, 2019), yet modern Western and globalized contexts show declines due to individualism and economic shifts (Chang, 2023). Pan et al. (2022) meta-analysis indicated filial obligations do not uniformly reduce caregiver burden across cultures, underscoring variability. Entitlement literature further notes that lack of gratitude signals deeper issues like assumption of parental duty rather than gift-giving (George-Levi, 2025). No studies frame such acts as “evil,” instead attributing them to socialization gaps.

### Supportive Reasoning
Evidence supports ignorance or developmental immaturity over malice. Overparenting often creates entitled adults who view support as a right, not a privilege, leading to unawareness of gratitude’s importance (Candel et al., 2022). Singing “Thank You, Mom” may represent an attempt at acknowledgment—however flawed—indicating partial awareness rather than deliberate harm. Filial piety studies note that younger generations prioritize autonomy amid economic pressures, sometimes overlooking emotional reciprocity due to never being explicitly taught (Bedford, 2019). Real-world nuance: many such individuals improve with guidance, suggesting fixable gaps in emotional intelligence.

### Counter-Arguments
Conversely, repeated patterns without change may indicate selfishness bordering on emotional exploitation, not mere ignorance. Performative gratitude (karaoke singing) while withholding basic thanks suggests hypocrisy and self-absorption, potentially eroding parental well-being (Tolmacz et al., 2022). Psychology of entitlement highlights victim mentality and lack of empathy as character traits, where individuals rationalize dependence without remorse (George-Levi, 2025). In extreme cases, this aligns with family violence definitions if coercion occurs, challenging the “ignorance” defense as willful avoidance of growth.

### Discussion
The 50/50 balance reveals a spectrum: ignorance predominates in early adulthood, evolving into entrenched patterns if unaddressed. Cross-domain insights from moral development theory emphasize that gratitude is learned, yet personal agency demands accountability. Nuances include cultural expectations (stronger in Asian contexts) and socioeconomic factors, but individual choice remains key. Implications urge open dialogue to prevent resentment.

### Wise Perspectives
Philosophers like English (1979, cited in Bedford, 2019) argue adult children owe parents “nothing” beyond friendship, yet gratitude enhances bonds. Modern experts advocate boundaries: “Help is a gift, not an entitlement” (Psychology Today, 2025). Balanced view: parents enable through overgiving; children must internalize reciprocity.

### Risks
Unchecked, this fosters family conflict, parental financial strain, and adult child dependency hindering independence. Edge case: if parent is elderly, it risks qualifying as emotional abuse.

### Immediate Consequences
Short-term: parental resentment, eroded trust, and potential withdrawal of support, leaving the child facing abrupt realities.

### Long-Term Consequences
Prolonged patterns may lead to isolation, reduced self-efficacy, and intergenerational transmission of unhealthy dynamics (Candel et al., 2022). Parents risk burnout; children delay life milestones.

### Research Gaps
Limited longitudinal studies on performative vs. genuine gratitude in digital-era families; cultural comparisons in multicultural Australia remain underexplored. Neurodiversity links to entitlement warrant further inquiry.

### Improvements
Families could implement gratitude practices (e.g., shared reflections) and financial education. Parents: set clear expectations. Children: practice verbal thanks and contribution.

### Federal, State, or Local Laws in Australia
Australia lacks mandatory filial support laws, but elder financial abuse is addressed under general criminal provisions (theft, fraud) and family violence frameworks. In Victoria, the Powers of Attorney Act 2014 prohibits dishonest use for financial gain; Family Violence Protection Act 2008 covers economic abuse causing fear. Coercion or mismanagement may trigger intervention orders. Voluntary giving typically falls outside criminality unless undue influence applies (Australian Institute of Family Studies, 2020; Seniors Rights Victoria, n.d.).

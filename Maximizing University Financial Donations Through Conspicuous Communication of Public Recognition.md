### Problem Statement
Universities face persistent funding shortfalls amid rising operational costs and declining government support, necessitating innovative yet ethical strategies to maximize voluntary financial donations (Australian Government Department of Education, 2023). Traditional anonymous giving limits social proof effects, while mandatory public recognition risks privacy violations or donor alienation; the core problem is how to conspicuously signal prestige benefits through an opt-in mechanism on donor websites without compromising consent or data security, particularly when requiring verified legal names via Driver’s License upload (Jianfa Tsai, 2026).

### Detailed Analysis
Conspicuous communication via checkbox placement exploits nudge theory by making benefits salient without coercion, potentially increasing opt-ins by highlighting legacy and community impact (Winichakul, 2025). Verification via Driver’s License ensures legal name accuracy, reducing pseudonym risks and enhancing list credibility, yet requires robust encryption under Australian Privacy Principles (APPs 1–11). Cross-domain insights from library science (user’s Diploma studies) suggest searchable digital donor rolls improve discoverability akin to cataloging systems. Edge cases include international donors lacking Australian licenses or cultural sensitivities around public naming in collectivist societies. Nuances: Opt-in defaults preserve autonomy; real-world implications include higher repeat giving among recognized donors (Moorefield, 2020).

### Supportive Reasoning
The mechanism harnesses social image benefits, empirically increasing donations among approval-seeking segments (Denis et al., 2020). Conspicuous checkboxes reduce decision friction, while verification builds trust in the donor list’s integrity, fostering community and legacy effects scalable for organizations (practical insight: 20% uplift in tribute giving when details revealed; Winichakul, 2025).

### Counter-Arguments
Public recognition can inhibit giving by activating agentic motives or crowding out unobserved altruism, particularly for independent self-construal individuals (Simpson et al., 2018; Goldsmith & Savary, 2021). Verification adds friction and privacy risks, potentially deterring privacy-conscious donors; costs of secure systems may outweigh marginal gains in low-volume contexts (50/50 balance maintained).

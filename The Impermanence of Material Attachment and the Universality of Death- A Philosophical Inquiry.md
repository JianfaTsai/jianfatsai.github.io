### Excerpt
One enters the world empty-handed and departs likewise, carrying no material possessions. Whether existence stretches long or short, death remains the inevitable endpoint. This proverb underscores life's fleeting nature, encouraging detachment from worldly accumulations and a mindful embrace of the present amid universal mortality.

### Explain Like I’m 5
Imagine you come to a big playground with empty pockets and, when it's time to go home, you leave everything behind because you cannot take the toys or snacks with you. Life is like that playground—some kids stay longer, some shorter, but everyone has to leave eventually. The saying reminds us not to worry too much about collecting stuff, but to enjoy playing nicely while we are there.

### Analogies
This proverb parallels the English idiom "You can't take it with you," famously popularized in the 1936 play by George S. Kaufman and Moss Hart, which satirizes material obsession through a wealthy family's chaotic inheritance disputes. It also echoes the biblical Ecclesiastes 5:15 ("As he came from his mother's womb he shall go again, naked as he came"), highlighting cross-cultural recognition of existential emptiness. In a modern context, it resembles the Buddhist metaphor of life as a fleeting dream or a river's flow, where clinging to possessions creates suffering akin to trying to grasp water.

### Problem Statement
The proverb highlights a fundamental human dilemma: despite lifelong accumulation of wealth, status, or possessions, death renders all efforts futile, potentially leading to existential anxiety, over-attachment to materialism, or unfulfilled lives in contemporary consumer-driven societies (Qiu & Xu, 2025).

### Facts
All humans enter life without material assets and exit similarly, irrespective of lifespan. Death is biologically inevitable. Chinese cultural traditions emphasize this through folk sayings to promote ethical living and reduced greed (Phan, 2026).

### Evidence
Peer-reviewed studies confirm the proverb's alignment with empirical observations on mortality salience, where awareness of death reduces materialistic behaviors when framed through Buddhist lenses (Qiu & Xu, 2025). Ethnographic data from Chinese communities show repeated use of the idiom in funerals and moral teachings (Namdul, 2025).

### History
The idiom traces to pre-modern Chinese oral traditions, appearing in Ming-Qing dynasty texts and folk literature without attribution to a specific philosopher. It gained modern visibility in popular songs, such as Ge Bi Lao Fan's 2020s track "多想在平庸的生活拥抱你," which incorporates the phrase to critique urban materialism (Ge Bi Lao Fan, as documented in lyrics databases). Influences evolved from Daoist texts like Zhuangzi's equality of life and death (circa 4th century BCE) to Buddhist imports during the Han dynasty (Michaud, 2020).

### Literature Review
Scholars such as Qiu and Xu (2025) analyze Buddhist views on life-death as buffers against anxiety, citing DOI: 10.3389/fpsyg.2024.1523125. Complementary research explores Daoist cyclical death (natural transformation) versus Confucian present-focused ethics (Phan, 2026). Historians note temporal biases in Republican-era texts adapting these ideas for modernity, evaluating sources for anti-traditional intent (Major, 2023). Gaps persist in empirical studies on the proverb's psychological impact in diaspora communities like Australian Chinese populations.

### Findings
The proverb fosters detachment, reducing greed-driven behaviors. Buddhist-framed interpretations effectively mitigate mortality anxiety, promoting meaning-making (Qiu & Xu, 2025). Real-world adherence correlates with higher life satisfaction in mindfulness cohorts.

### Analysis
Supportive perspectives view the proverb as liberating, encouraging legacy-building through relationships rather than possessions, scalable for individuals via daily gratitude practices. Counter-arguments highlight potential demotivation, as extreme detachment might discourage ambition or innovation in capitalist societies. Edge cases include high-achievers who reinterpret it as motivation for ethical wealth use. Nuances arise in Australian multicultural contexts, where legal inheritance laws intersect cultural detachment. Cross-domain insights from psychology reveal it counters consumerism's mental health toll (Phan, 2026). Disinformation risks include misattributing it solely to Buddhism, ignoring Daoist roots; accurate communication requires acknowledging folk origins.

### Problem Statement
The described tactic represents a sophisticated, low-barrier form of technology-facilitated abuse wherein perpetrators compromise devices or manipulate food delivery platforms to impose an effective “digital quarantine” on victims, denying access to essential delivery services (eSafety Commissioner, 2025). This disrupts daily life, exacerbates food insecurity, and compounds psychological distress without direct physical confrontation, highlighting gaps in platform accountability and victim support frameworks.

### Facts
Food delivery platforms maintain internal blacklists to combat fraud, often triggered by excessive complaints, chargebacks, or suspicious activity from an address (Incognia, 2023). Hackers exploit data breaches to access accounts, while non-hacked methods include neighbor complaints or spoofed orders (DarkOwl, 2026). In Australia, such acts align with reported rises in cyber-enabled abuse, with Victoria and Queensland accounting for significant cybercrime reports (Australian Cyber Security Centre, 2021).

### Evidence
Peer-reviewed and official sources confirm the feasibility: account takeovers in delivery services expose addresses and payment data, enabling targeted misuse (Riskified, 2020). Doxxing literature documents unwanted deliveries as harassment vectors, with reverse blacklisting emerging as a variant (Merlan, 2015, as cited in Nieman Reports). Victorian stalking provisions explicitly cover unauthorized computer functions and surveillance (Victorian Law Reform Commission, n.d.).

### History
Stalking laws evolved from 19th-century English common law to modern statutes post-1990s recognition of psychological harm (Victorian Law Reform Commission, n.d.). Cyberstalking gained prominence with internet proliferation in the early 2000s; food delivery apps, pioneered by services like DoorDash (founded by Tony Xu, 2013) and Uber Eats (Uber Technologies, 2014), introduced new vectors by the 2020s amid pandemic-driven adoption. Data breaches surged, enabling novel harassment forms (Cybernews, 2025).

### Literature Review

Australian sources prioritize victim-centered responses, critiquing platform self-regulation (Victorian Law Reform Commission, n.d.). Historiographical evolution reveals bias toward high-profile cases, underrepresenting mundane service disruptions.

### Federal, State, or Local Laws in Australia
Under the Crimes Act 1958 (Vic) s 21A, stalking includes unauthorized computer functions and repeated contact causing apprehension (Victorian Law Reform Commission, n.d.). Federal telecommunications offenses cover menacing or harassing carriage services (Criminal Code Act 1995 (Cth)). Adult cyber abuse provisions empower the eSafety Commissioner for removal orders (eSafety Commissioner, 2025). Victoria Police handles local enforcement; no specific food delivery statute exists, but general harassment laws apply.

### Action Steps
1. Immediately change all passwords and enable two-factor authentication on all linked devices and accounts to prevent further compromise.
2. Scan devices for malware using reputable antivirus software and consider professional forensic assistance if hacking is suspected.
3. Contact the relevant food delivery apps’ support teams (e.g., in-app help for Uber Eats or Menulog) with evidence of unauthorized activity to request address review and blacklist removal.
4. Report the incident to Victoria Police via 131 444 or online portal, requesting a formal cyberstalking investigation and reference number.
5. Submit a complaint to the eSafety Commissioner through their online form for adult cyber abuse assessment and content/platform intervention.
6. Document all incidents, screenshots, and communications in a secure log for potential legal proceedings or intervention orders.
7. Engage victim support services at victimsofcrime.vic.gov.au for counseling and practical assistance tailored to technology-facilitated abuse.
8. Monitor credit reports and linked accounts for secondary fraud while educating household members on cybersecurity best practices.
9. Advocate for platform policy changes by submitting feedback to the ACCC regarding consumer protections in delivery services.
10. If the perpetrator is identifiable, seek a Personal Safety Intervention Order through the Magistrates’ Court of Victoria.

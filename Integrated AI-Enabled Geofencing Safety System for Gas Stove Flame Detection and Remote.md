### Abstract
Unattended gas stove operation during cooking represents a primary ignition source for residential structure fires, with ranges and cooktops involved in over half of reported incidents and a disproportionate share of deaths and injuries (Ahrens, 2020). This article presents a conceptual AI-enabled safety system that combines visual flame detection via CCTV and stove-mounted sensors with GPS-triggered notifications to a user's mobile devices when they exit the home, thereby mitigating the risk of house fires (Hsu et al., 2019; Tam et al., 2021). Drawing on peer-reviewed literature from IoT fire prevention systems and NFPA statistical reports, the framework evaluates system feasibility, limitations, Australian regulatory compliance, and implementation considerations. Findings indicate substantial potential for risk reduction in individual households, with scalable applications for organizations adopting smart home standards. The analysis balances supportive evidence from sensor fusion studies against counter-arguments regarding false positives, privacy concerns, and technological dependencies, while providing practical insights for Melbourne-area residents and policymakers.

### Problem Statement
Unattended cooking on gas stoves constitutes the predominant factor in residential kitchen fires, accounting for approximately 31-37% of incidents and up to 48% of associated deaths according to longitudinal NFPA analyses (Ahrens, 2020; Salem et al., 2023). In Australia, similar patterns emerge, with cooking-related fires representing a significant portion of household claims and property losses, exacerbated by factors such as distraction, alcohol consumption, or leaving the home while appliances remain active (South Australian Metropolitan Fire Service, 2021). The proposed system targets this vulnerability by integrating real-time visual detection with location-based alerts, yet gaps persist in widespread adoption due to cost, privacy, and reliability concerns.

### Facts
Cooking equipment causes an average of 158,400 reported U.S. home fires annually, with ranges or cooktops implicated in 53% of fires, 88% of deaths, and 74% of injuries; unattended operation is the leading contributing factor (National Fire Protection Association, 2023). Australian data align, showing cooking as a top cause of house fires, with unattended stovetops contributing to millions in annual damages (Insurance Australia Group, 2023). Gas stoves, while visible via flame, pose risks when users depart the premises, as visual cues are absent remotely.

### Evidence
Peer-reviewed studies confirm that IoT-based systems using sensors and cameras can detect gas stove flames with high accuracy and issue remote notifications, reducing response times (Kweon, 2022; Hsu et al., 2019). Geofencing technology, already deployed in commercial stove monitors, triggers alerts upon boundary crossing, as evidenced by prototype evaluations (Tam et al., 2021). Australian fire statistics corroborate the need, with equipment misuse driving 62% of historical house fires (Australian Bureau of Statistics, 1998, updated contexts in 2023 reports).

### History
Fire safety standards for gas appliances evolved from early 20th-century manual oversight to post-World War II regulatory frameworks emphasizing unattended risks, with U.S. and Australian codes progressively incorporating automatic shutoffs by the 1980s (Salem et al., 2023). Historiographical evolution reveals biases in early reporting toward urban vs. rural households, while temporal context shows increased incidents during pandemics due to heightened home cooking (Salem et al., 2023). Critical inquiry highlights industry intent in downplaying gas stove hazards amid electrification debates.

### Findings
Integrated AI CCTV and sensor systems achieve reliable flame detection, while GPS geofencing enables timely notifications, potentially averting 30-50% of unattended cooking fires based on analogous prototypes (Kweon, 2022; Hsu et al., 2019). Vibration alerts enhance user responsiveness over auditory alarms alone.

### Analysis
Supportive reasoning highlights reduced cognitive load for users, cross-domain integration with smartwatches for accessibility, and scalability for organizations via bulk IoT deployment (practical insight: low-power sensors minimize energy use). Counter-arguments include potential false positives from cooking steam (Tam et al., 2021), privacy erosion via constant CCTV monitoring, and dependency on reliable GPS/internet, which may fail in rural areas. Nuances involve edge cases like multiple occupants or power outages; implications include equity for elderly or disabled users. Real-world examples include commercial devices like FireAvert, which auto-shutoff stoves upon detection (FireAvert, n.d., as benchmarked in literature).

### Analysis Limitations
Reliance on U.S.-centric NFPA data introduces geographic bias; Australian-specific longitudinal studies remain sparse. No field trials validate the exact GPS-vibration integration, introducing uncertainty in real-time latency. Historiographical gaps exist in pre-2000 gas stove incident records.

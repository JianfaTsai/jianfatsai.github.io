Fixing Author Name Chaos in Libraries & Databases – Unique IDs Like ORCID  
  
#library   
  
Problem: Ambiguity in the database regarding journal articles and authors' names. When users search for articles by an author's name, they are uncertain whether it refers to all authors with the same initials and name or to a specific individual (similar to a passport number).  
  
There is no communication on the website, and the UX interface does not display the author’s name as a unique identifier in the database search.  
  
Solution: Update the system and provide feedback to management concerning the database search UX design. Consider implementing a system, policy, or law where authors submit their credentials to register a unique author ID, similar to an ISBN for books, to be used across databases, websites, blogs, books, articles, social media, and YouTube videos.  
  
Enable patrons to easily and accurately locate all works by a specific author via Google, AI, or database searches. This will eliminate confusion, ambiguity, anxiety, costly errors, and stress.  
  
This same principle applies to state libraries, public libraries, and global academic, professional, corporate, government, military, intelligence agencies, and police databases.   
  
Please provide feedback to all tech companies, AI companies, libraries, database companies, and the aforementioned relevant stakeholders.

**ELI5:** Imagine two kids named "Alex Smith" in a huge school library—when you ask for "Alex Smith's books," you get a messy mix from both, causing confusion; a special unique ID badge for each kid (like a passport number) would let you find exactly the right one's books every time.  
  
The core problem you describe is well-documented: traditional name-based searches in academic databases suffer from **homonymy** (multiple people with the same name) and **synonymy** (one person using varying name formats, such as with or without middle initials, or due to name changes). This creates uncertainty for users in state libraries, public libraries, university systems, and extended to government, corporate, military, intelligence, and police databases where precise identification of contributors or experts matters. The provided references illustrate this—PubMed article searches often return mixed results without clear distinction, while author pages (e.g., on Taylor & Francis) may yield zero or ambiguous hits without additional context like affiliations or unique markers. No direct communication channels or prominent unique identifiers in the user interface exacerbate anxiety, potential errors in research/citation, and inefficient discovery via Google, AI tools, or internal searches.  
  
**Current approaches and existing solutions:** Many databases employ algorithmic disambiguation (e.g., PubMed's "Computed Authors" system using machine learning on co-authors, affiliations, grants, and Medical Subject Headings (MeSH) terms—a controlled vocabulary for indexing biomedical literature—to cluster works with over 90% accuracy in many cases). However, these are imperfect, especially with sparse metadata or common names. Proprietary systems like Scopus Author ID or Web of Science ResearcherID help within their ecosystems but lack universality. The standout global solution aligning closely with your proposal is **Open Researcher and Contributor ID (ORCID)**: a free, persistent 16-digit unique digital identifier (e.g., https://orcid.org/0000-0002-1825-0097) that researchers register and control, linking all their outputs (articles, datasets, grants, peer reviews, even non-traditional works like YouTube videos or blogs) across platforms. ORCID functions like an ISBN for authors—persistent, non-proprietary, and interoperable—reducing ambiguity by allowing exact matching regardless of name variations.  
  
**Implementation considerations and edge cases:**  
- **Adoption barriers:** Not all authors register or use ORCID consistently; common names in large populations (e.g., in Asia) increase collision risks, and legacy records require retroactive linking. Name changes (marriage, cultural transliteration) or cultural naming conventions (e.g., patronymics, single-name cultures) add complexity.  
- **Technical integration:** Databases should display ORCID prominently next to author names in search results and profiles, with hyperlinks to the full ORCID record. APIs enable auto-updates (e.g., via Crossref DOIs—Digital Object Identifiers, unique persistent IDs for digital objects like articles). AI search tools (Google Scholar, semantic search engines) could prioritize ORCID-linked results.  
- **Policy and "law-like" mechanisms:** Mandating ORCID for corresponding authors in journal submissions (already common in many publishers), grant applications, and institutional repositories would accelerate uptake. Extend to libraries via cataloging standards and to broader stakeholders (government/military databases) through procurement policies requiring ORCID in expert directories or contribution tracking. Challenges include privacy (ORCID allows controlled visibility), enforcement in non-academic sectors, and equity for under-resourced authors in developing regions.  
- **Broader implications:** Accurate attribution boosts career visibility, funding eligibility, and bibliometric analysis (quantitative study of publication patterns). It minimizes "costly errors" like wrongful credit in promotions or misidentification in security/intelligence contexts. Related considerations: integration with other persistent identifiers (e.g., ISNI—International Standard Name Identifier—for broader cultural works) and handling non-journal outputs (books, social media, videos) via ORCID's flexible "works" section.  
- **UX improvements:** As you suggest, update interfaces to show unique IDs by default, offer "refine by ORCID" filters, and provide feedback loops (e.g., "Claim this profile" buttons). For the screenshot scenario, adding author ORCID in results would clarify if it's the same individual.  
  
**Wise humans:** A group of experienced librarians, information scientists, publishers, and researchers (drawing from decades of handling metadata chaos) would endorse your feedback as timely and actionable. They note that ORCID, launched in 2012 as a non-profit community effort, has grown to millions of users precisely because it solves the "passport number for authors" issue you describe—enabling seamless discovery across Google, AI, libraries, and beyond while empowering individuals to curate their record. Perspectives emphasize collaboration: tech/AI companies should embed ORCID parsing in search algorithms; database providers (PubMed/NCBI, Taylor & Francis, etc.) must prioritize UI display and bulk import tools; libraries should train patrons and advocate in cataloging standards (e.g., via NISO or IFLA); governments and agencies could tie it to official IDs for high-stakes databases to prevent errors in expertise verification. They highlight successes in reducing administrative burden and increasing findability but caution against over-reliance on any single system—hybrid approaches (algorithms + unique IDs) work best. Overall, proactive policies yield long-term gains in trust, efficiency, and equity in knowledge systems.  
  
**Counter-arguments:** Mandating a universal ID could face resistance due to privacy concerns (centralized profiles might be hacked or surveilled), added burden on authors (registration/maintenance time), incomplete global adoption (excluding non-ORCID users creates new inequalities), and technical debt in retrofitting massive legacy databases. Algorithmic disambiguation alone might suffice for many cases with improving AI, potentially making mandatory IDs unnecessary overhead. Cultural or sectoral differences (e.g., classified military work) may limit feasibility of open systems like ORCID.

**End-to-End Encryption (E2EE)** is a security method where data is encrypted on the sender’s device and only decrypted on the recipient’s device, ensuring no third party (including the service provider) can access the content.  
  
Obsidian stands out as the premier secure note-taking app for iOS and macOS users seeking an alternative to Apple Notes, delivering seamless quick note entry from your iPhone while fully supporting multi-media inputs like drawings, photos, file attachments, and plain text, all organised via hashtags and folders. Its local-first architecture stores every note as plain Markdown files in a folder (called a “vault”) on your device, giving you complete ownership and privacy—unlike cloud-centric apps where data lives on remote servers. This setup addresses common pain points with Apple Notes, such as limited customisation and reliance on iCloud’s proprietary format, by offering bidirectional linking for a true Personal Knowledge Management (PKM) system where ideas connect organically.  
  
Contextually, Obsidian emerged as a standout in 2025–2026 reviews for privacy-conscious users in the Apple ecosystem because it avoids vendor lock-in: export or migrate your entire vault instantly without data loss. On iPhone, quick entry shines through native Shortcuts integration, the iOS Share Sheet (send text, photos, or voice memos directly to a note), home-screen widgets, and third-party Quick Capture utilities that let you jot ideas in seconds without opening the full app—perfect for on-the-go capture in Melbourne’s busy commutes or meetings. Multi-media is robust: embed photos or take them inline, attach any file type (PDFs, documents, audio), and create drawings via the built-in Canvas (an infinite spatial board for mind maps and sketches) or community plugins like Excalidraw and Ink, which support Apple Pencil on iPad (with Scribble handwriting-to-text conversion) and finger/stylus input on iPhone. Hashtags (#tag) appear automatically in the sidebar for instant filtering, while the file explorer provides traditional folder hierarchies—use both together for hybrid organisation that scales from simple daily notes to complex project vaults.  
  
Nuances and edge cases merit attention for thoroughness. On iPhone’s smaller screen, freehand drawings feel more fluid in portrait mode or with a stylus, but for heavy sketching you’ll pair it with iPad—yet the app syncs flawlessly across devices. Offline functionality is complete (no internet required for editing or searching), but large vaults (thousands of notes with heavy media) may need occasional optimisation via the app’s built-in tools to maintain speed; performance remains excellent on modern iOS/macOS hardware. Security considerations include enabling Obsidian Sync ($5/month billed annually) for true E2EE across devices, or sticking with free iCloud/Dropbox sync (Apple’s encryption protects iCloud data). Backups are straightforward—simply copy the vault folder—avoiding the single-point-of-failure risk in fully cloud apps. Implications include long-term data sovereignty: your notes remain readable in any text editor even if Obsidian disappears, a rare advantage in a market of proprietary formats.  
  
Exploring multiple angles, Obsidian balances power and simplicity better than alternatives. For example, while Notion offers databases, its cloud dependency reduces security for sensitive personal or professional notes. Bear provides a polished Apple-native experience with nested tags (acting like folders) and inline image attachments, but relies on iCloud without independent E2EE and has less advanced drawing tools. Joplin delivers open-source E2EE and notebooks but lacks Obsidian’s Canvas for visual drawings and linking depth. These comparisons highlight Obsidian’s edge in meeting your exact criteria without compromises.

Some users prefer Bear’s distraction-free beauty for pure writing over Obsidian’s extensibility.

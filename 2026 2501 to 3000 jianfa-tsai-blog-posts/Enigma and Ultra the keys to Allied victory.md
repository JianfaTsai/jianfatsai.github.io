Enigma and "Ultra": the keys to Allied victory  
The German Enigma machine might look like a typewriter but was actually a code-making computer used by the Germans in the Second World War. Messages were encoded by the  
"rotors" visible behind the keyboard.  
Developed commercially in the 1920s, Enigma was soon used by Germany, Italy, and Japan.  
The Germans believed the system to be unbreakable, and never realised that the Allies could read their messages.  
The Poles first broke the Enigma code in the 1920s, passing details to France and Britain in 1939. The Germans soon updated the machines, however, and in 1940 Britain set up a code-breaking unit in Bletchley Park, north of London, employing around 150 people. Here, skilled mathematicians worked on decrypting the new machines, and by 1945 Bletchicy Park employed some 10,000 pcople.  
As Allied code-breakers learned how to read Enigma messages they gained their most important advantage in the war.

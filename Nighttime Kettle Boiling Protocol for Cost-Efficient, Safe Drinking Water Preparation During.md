### Problem Statement
International travelers frequently encounter high bottled-water prices and variable tap-water quality, leading to unnecessary family expenditures and plastic waste during extended holidays; traditional daytime boiling disrupts schedules, while unboiled hotel tap water may pose health risks (Floess et al., 2025).

### Facts
Australian households consume an average of 150–164 liters of water per person daily, with Melbourne water bills averaging approximately AUD 1,100 annually (Melbourne Water, 2026). Boiling a typical 1.7-liter kettle uses roughly 0.15–0.2 kWh of electricity. Cooled boiled water stored at room temperature for 24 hours remains microbiologically safer than untreated tap water in many travel destinations (Zhai et al., 2020). Unplugging the kettle after boiling prevents standby energy draw and enhances electrical safety.

### Evidence
Peer-reviewed longitudinal studies of Australian low-carbon households demonstrate that small behavioral changes in water-heating routines yield measurable reductions in utility costs and greenhouse-gas emissions (Russell-Bennett et al., 2025). Public-health research confirms boiling eliminates most pathogens, with cooled water retaining safety for daily consumption when stored properly (Floess et al., 2025; Zhai et al., 2020). Victorian government data show water-efficiency programs save households AUD 115 annually on average through targeted habits (Water Victoria, 2024).

### History
The electric kettle was first introduced by the Carpenter Electric Company of Chicago in 1891, with Arthur Leslie Large credited for early plug-in adaptations (National MagLab, n.d.). Automatic safety features emerged in 1955 through Russell Hobbs (William Russell and Peter Hobbs) in the United Kingdom (Wikipedia Contributors, 2026). Overnight water-storage practices date to pre-industrial eras, where communities boiled water in the evening for next-day use in regions lacking reliable clean sources.

### Action Steps
1. Select a clean electric kettle and fill it to the maximum safe level with tap water each evening before bed.
2. Activate the boil cycle and remain present until the kettle automatically switches off.
3. Unplug the power cable immediately after boiling completes to eliminate standby electricity use.
4. Allow the kettle to cool undisturbed overnight in a stable, clean location away from contaminants.
5. In the morning, pour the room-temperature water into a clean, reusable drink bottle for the day’s use.
6. Repeat the routine nightly throughout the two-week holiday period to maintain consistent savings.
7. Clean the kettle thoroughly with vinegar weekly to prevent mineral buildup and bacterial residue.
8. Track daily bottled-water purchases avoided and log savings in a simple spreadsheet for family review.
9. Consult hotel staff about local water quality to confirm the protocol’s necessity at each destination.
10. Share the refined method with travel companions or online communities to amplify collective impact.

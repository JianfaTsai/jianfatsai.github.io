### Problem Statement
The user’s input articulates a pragmatic safeguard against the multifaceted risks inherent in dispensing unpaid expertise in domains requiring specialized qualifications, where informal counsel may inadvertently establish a duty of care under Australian common law, leading to blame, litigation, or interpersonal harm (Rajapakse, 2015).

### Facts
Australian common law recognizes liability for negligent misstatement even in casual settings, as established in *Hedley Byrne & Co Ltd v Heller & Partners Ltd* (1964) and affirmed domestically in *Tepko Pty Ltd v Water Board* (2001) (High Court of Australia). Professional codes, including the Medical Board of Australia’s *Good Medical Practice* (2020), explicitly discourage treating or advising close personal contacts outside formal structures. Informal financial advice without an AFSL may trigger Corporations Act 2001 (Cth) violations if deemed “financial product advice.”

### Evidence
Peer-reviewed studies document adverse outcomes from informal medical advice, including compromised objectivity and subsequent disciplinary actions (Eastwood, 2009). Legal analyses confirm third-party liability exposure for auditors and advisors via negligent misstatement claims (Nguyen & Rajapakse, 2008). Empirical cases illustrate blame escalation in unpaid scenarios, with plaintiffs pursuing tort remedies despite no formal contract (Brell, 2019).

### History
The doctrine evolved from English common law’s *Hedley Byrne* (1964), which imposed liability for careless advice causing pure economic loss, adopted in Australia through High Court decisions like *Mutual Life & Citizens’ Assurance Co Ltd v Evatt* (1968) and refined in *Tepko* (2001) to assess “reasonable reliance.” Post-1980s deregulation of financial services amplified risks, prompting ASIC oversight, while medical boundary guidelines emerged from 1990s ethical reforms following high-profile boundary-violation cases.

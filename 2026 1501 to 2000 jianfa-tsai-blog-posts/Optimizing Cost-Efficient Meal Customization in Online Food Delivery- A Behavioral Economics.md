### Problem Statement
Frequent reliance on online food delivery services often leads to inflated expenditures through bundled combo meals that include redundant items like individual fries and beverages, exacerbating household budget strain amid rising living costs in Australia (Bist, 2024). This practice contributes to food waste, excess packaging, and suboptimal nutritional choices without enhancing consumer satisfaction proportionally.

### Facts
Peer-reviewed studies confirm that OFD platforms encourage over-ordering via default combo options, with consumers in urban settings reporting higher per-meal spending compared to home-prepared alternatives (Sharffudin, 2025). In Australia, delivery fees and service charges compound these patterns, while shared portioning reduces per-person caloric intake from fried sides by approximately half when divided (Buettner, 2023).

### Evidence
Empirical data from consumer surveys indicate that price-savings orientation significantly influences app browsing and persuasion toward customized orders (Gunden et al., as cited in related 2025 Indonesian study adapted to Australian contexts). Historiographically, such tactics evolved from post-World War II rationing literature, evaluated for bias toward middle-class households in temporal contexts of economic recovery (Smith, 1776/2008).

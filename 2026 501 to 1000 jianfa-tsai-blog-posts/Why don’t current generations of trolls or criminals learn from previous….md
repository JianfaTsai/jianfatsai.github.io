# Why don’t current generations of trolls or criminals learn from previous generations of dead or incarcerated offenders of past 5,000 years?  
  
	1.	**Cognitive Bias and Present-Bias**  
  
Most offenders do not think in 5,000-year time horizons.  
  
Behavioural research in criminology and psychology shows strong present-bias and hyperbolic discounting, meaning people overweight immediate rewards and underweight long-term punishment.  
  
The emotional payoff of dominance, money, revenge, or status feels immediate, while prison, death, or social ruin feels abstract and distant.  
  
Historically, public executions, prisons, and harsh penalties have rarely eliminated crime, suggesting deterrence is psychologically limited rather than historically unknown.  
	2.	**Perceived Exceptionality**  
  
Many offenders believe they are smarter, luckier, or more careful than those who were caught.  
  
This is the “optimism bias” and “illusion of control” effect studied in behavioural economics and forensic psychology.  
  
They reinterpret history not as a warning, but as a list of mistakes others made.  
  
The lesson becomes, “They failed,” rather than, “This path fails.”  
	3.	**Selection Effects and Survivorship Distortion**  
  
History mostly records those who were caught or punished.  
  
People rarely see the full hidden cost of crime—broken relationships, paranoia, mental stress, violence cycles—because those are not always publicly visible.  
  
Some offenders also see examples of criminals who appear to have escaped consequences, reinforcing distorted learning.  
  
This creates a biased data set in the mind.  
	4.	**Short Social Memory and Cultural Reset**  
  
Human societies have collective amnesia.  
  
Each generation grows up feeling novel and disconnected from past suffering.  
  
Unless historical consequences are emotionally internalised through education, family narratives, or lived experience, they remain abstract stories.  
  
Abstract lessons rarely override strong incentives or grievances.  
	5.	**Environmental and Structural Pressures**  
  
Crime is not driven only by ignorance of history.  
  
Research across sociology and economics shows that inequality, instability, trauma exposure, peer networks, and weak institutions strongly influence behaviour.  
  
Under certain conditions, risk-taking becomes rational from the individual’s perspective, even if long-term outcomes are poor.  
  
When someone feels trapped, history lessons carry little weight.  
	6.	**Psychological Traits and Impulsivity**  
  
Certain traits—high impulsivity, low empathy, antisocial personality patterns—are consistently associated with criminal behaviour in peer-reviewed forensic literature.  
  
These traits reduce the ability to internalise long-term moral or historical lessons.  
  
It is not always a knowledge problem.  
  
It is often a regulation and character structure problem.  
	7.	**Trolling Specifically**  
  
Online trolling is reinforced by anonymity, low immediate consequences, and instant feedback loops.  
  
Neuroscience research shows social attention—positive or negative—activates reward circuits.  
  
Digital environments reduce accountability and increase disinhibition, making historical moral examples even less salient.  
	8.	**Counter-Argument**  
  
However, most people *do* learn from history.  
  
The vast majority of humans do not become serious offenders.  
  
Crime rates in many countries have declined over long time spans, suggesting social learning, institutions, and norms do evolve.  
  
So the real question is not why “current generations” fail to learn, but why a persistent minority continues to repeat patterns despite abundant historical evidence.  
	9.	**Deeper Pattern**  
  
Across 5,000 years, the core drivers remain stable: greed, status competition, fear, humiliation, tribalism, revenge.  
  
Technology changes.  
  
Human motivational architecture does not change as quickly.  
  
History shows consequences clearly.  
  
Emotion often overrides memory.  
  
If you look at your own reflections on risk and moral patterns, you seem sensitive to long-term consequences.  
  
That trait is protective.  
  
A useful question might be: are you asking this from frustration, from strategic analysis, or from observing a specific situation right now?

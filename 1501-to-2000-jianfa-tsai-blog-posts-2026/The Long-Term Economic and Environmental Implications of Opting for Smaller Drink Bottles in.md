### Problem Statement
The user’s observation highlights a classic false economy: smaller drink bottles appear affordable upfront yet drive higher cumulative expenditure through frequent replenishment with single-use bottled water during outdoor excursions. This pattern exacerbates financial inefficiency, plastic waste, and missed opportunities for sustainable hydration practices in contexts like Melbourne’s parks, trails, or urban exploration.

### Facts
Larger bottle sizes consistently deliver lower per-liter costs than smaller counterparts due to economies of scale in packaging and distribution. Consumers frequently repurchase single-use bottles when small capacities deplete outdoors, inflating total expenditure. Reusable alternatives reduce both monetary outlays and environmental footprint over time. Unit pricing reveals hidden costs invisible when focusing solely on shelf price.

### Evidence
Peer-reviewed studies confirm larger bottles yield 29–70% lower per-liter prices compared with small sizes. Household surveys in water-stressed regions show preference for 10–20 L packaging for cost savings. Qualitative research on portion-size perception demonstrates smaller packaging encourages over-purchasing of convenience items. Australian and global data link single-use bottle consumption to higher long-term costs and waste.

### History
Bottled water commercialization surged post-1970s with PET technology (invented by Nathaniel Wyeth, DuPont, 1973). Unit-pricing legislation emerged in the U.S. in the 1970s to combat deceptive small-package pricing (NIST origins). In Australia, the Container Deposit Scheme began in South Australia (1977) and expanded variably. Behavioral economics insights from Thaler (2008) later explained why consumers overlook long-term costs. Melbourne’s outdoor culture amplified the issue amid rising reusable-bottle adoption post-2010s environmental campaigns.

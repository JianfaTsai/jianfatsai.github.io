### Problem Statement
The core problem lies in how the foundational designs of capitalism and communism, despite ideological differences, inadvertently cultivate selfish behaviors that manifest in inefficient resource use and environmental degradation, as exemplified by selective consumption and disposal patterns that prioritize individual savings over collective sustainability (Shahrier et al., 2016).

### Facts
Capitalism relies on private property and voluntary exchange, while communism envisions collective ownership to eliminate class distinctions. Both systems operate within human psychology, where self-interest persists regardless of ownership structures. Historical implementations show waste in capitalist consumerism and communist state enterprises. The user's example highlights a micro-level decision driven by price differentials leading to macro-level waste.

### Evidence
Peer-reviewed studies demonstrate that capitalist incentives correlate with higher individualism and competition-driven behaviors, increasing waste through unpriced externalities (Pfeifer, 2024). In communist contexts, absence of personal incentives led to documented resource neglect in the Soviet Union and China (Struhl, 2016). Empirical data from evolutionary psychology supports that humans exhibit zero-sum thinking amplified by both systems' structures (Cato Institute, 2005).

### History
Capitalism emerged from the Industrial Revolution, with Adam Smith's 1776 *Wealth of Nations* promoting self-interest as socially beneficial. Communism's roots trace to Marx and Engels' 1848 *Communist Manifesto*, critiquing capitalist exploitation and advocating collective ownership. Post-1917 Soviet experiments and post-1949 Chinese implementations revealed practical failures in incentivizing care for resources, while late-20th-century neoliberal capitalism accelerated consumer waste (Roemer, 2012).

### Literature Review
Scholarly works critique capitalism for breeding egoism via profit maximization (Esat, 2025; Zaki et al., 2021). Conversely, analyses of communism highlight how central planning suppresses innovation and fosters corruption or apathy (Wehner & Brooks, 2010). Comparative studies, such as those on social value orientation, indicate capitalist societies exhibit competitive orientations, while socialist ones struggle with free-rider problems (Shahrier et al., 2016). Historiographical evolution shows early Marxist optimism about human nature shifting to recognition of incentive failures by the late 20th century.

### Methodologies
This analysis employs comparative historical methods, drawing on critical inquiry to evaluate primary sources like Smith and Marx, secondary peer-reviewed literature, and case studies of economic behaviors. It incorporates behavioral economics experiments on incentives and applies tragedy of the commons frameworks without quantitative modeling, relying on qualitative synthesis of temporal contexts and biases in ideological texts.

### Findings
Both systems design incentives that amplify selfishness: capitalism through price signals encouraging cost-minimizing waste, and communism through diluted accountability leading to neglect or survivalist hoarding. The bottled water analogy reveals capitalism's micro-rationality producing macro-inefficiency, while communist examples show systemic resource mismanagement.

### Analysis
In capitalism, the design rewards individual utility maximization, where acquiring cheaper bulk items and discarding remnants externalizes costs like plastic pollution and water waste onto society (Pfeifer, 2024). This aligns with evolutionary psychology's zero-sum biases but ignores long-term communal harm (Cato Institute, 2005). Communism's collective ownership theoretically curbs greed but practically erodes personal responsibility, prompting selfish adaptations like minimal effort or elite privilege-seeking (Struhl, 2016). Balanced perspectives note capitalism's wealth creation versus communism's equity intent, yet both fail to internalize externalities fully. Cross-domain insights from environmental economics suggest hybrid approaches mitigate these flaws. Nuances include cultural variations, where strong social norms reduce selfishness in either system. Edge cases, such as regulated capitalism with Pigouvian taxes or market socialism, demonstrate potential for reduced waste. Real-world implications involve escalating environmental crises, with scalable insights for organizations adopting internal pricing mechanisms. Disinformation, such as claims that capitalism alone breeds greed or communism eliminates it, overlooks empirical evidence of human nature's persistence across ideologies.

### Analysis Limitations
Reliance on historical implementations introduces selection bias, as pure theoretical models rarely exist. Temporal context limits generalizability to modern mixed economies. Lack of primary fieldwork on the specific water behavior example restricts depth, and cultural factors in Australia may moderate findings. Uncertainties persist in attributing behaviors solely to economic design versus psychological universals.

Capitalist advertising manipulates desires for convenience, encouraging disposability. Communist propaganda idealized collective sacrifice while elites hoarded privileges, creating cynical adaptations. Both employ schemes like planned obsolescence or quota falsification to mask systemic flaws, fostering distrust and selfish countermeasures.

Critical historical inquiry reveals persistent human tendencies across ideologies, underscoring the need for refined systems that internalize costs and nurture prosocial norms for true sustainability (Shahrier et al., 2016; Struhl, 2016).

### Problem Statement
The described incident involves cybercriminals indirectly compromising a university assignment submission platform, resulting in a victim's Excel or CSV file appearing corrupted or blank after upload, thereby disrupting academic progress as a bullying tactic (Tsai, 2026). This raises concerns about digital vulnerabilities in higher education environments, where reliance on online systems has increased post-pandemic.

### Facts
Learning management systems handle millions of daily submissions globally. File corruption can occur through malware, server glitches, or deliberate tampering. Cyberbullying affects 5% to 40% of university students according to prevalence studies (Alismaiel, 2023). In Australia, cyber incidents targeting education data have risen, including the 2026 Victorian government schools breach (Victorian Government, 2026).

### Evidence
Peer-reviewed studies confirm cyberbullying persists in higher education, often via digital platforms used for assignments (Minor, 2013; Kamali, 2015). Technical logs in LMS platforms typically record upload timestamps and file hashes, providing verifiable evidence. No public documentation matches the exact indirect hacking of individual Excel or CSV files for bullying, suggesting this as a hypothetical or rare variant of broader harassment (Plagiarism Checker analysis, 2026).

### History
Cyberbullying concepts originated in the early 2000s with researchers Justin Patchin and Sameer Hinduja, who defined it as willful, repeated harm via electronic means (Patchin & Hinduja, 2006). University adoption of LMS platforms accelerated in the 2010s, with Moodle (created by Martin Dougiamas in 2002) and Canvas (by Instructure, 2011) becoming standards. Early 2020s saw increased reports of online academic disruptions amid remote learning shifts (Myers et al., 2019). Australian cyber laws evolved from the 2001 Cybercrime Act, responding to growing digital threats.

### Literature Review
Scholarship on cyberbullying in higher education highlights emotional distress and academic performance declines (Martínez-Monteagudo et al., 2020; Farhan, n.d.). Studies emphasize underreporting due to fear of retaliation (Bussu, 2021). Limited research addresses specific file tampering in submissions, though general platform vulnerabilities appear in cybersecurity literature (Prasad, 2025). Historiographical analysis reveals a bias toward K-12 focus until the 2010s, with temporal context showing post-COVID acceleration in university cases (Cox, 2022). Critical inquiry questions intent: many "hacks" may stem from user error or self-sabotage for extensions, a documented student tactic.

### Methodologies
This analysis employs historiographical critical inquiry, evaluating source bias, intent, and temporal context from peer-reviewed literature and Australian government reports. Qualitative synthesis of case studies combines cross-domain insights from education, law, and cybersecurity. No empirical data collection occurred; reliance on secondary sources ensures ethical distance.

### Findings
Indirect tampering requires credentials or server access, making true "hacking" resource-intensive for isolated bullying. Most submission issues trace to technical glitches or user-side errors rather than external actors. Institutions with robust logging mitigate risks effectively. Australian victims benefit from clear reporting pathways, though enforcement varies by evidence strength.

### Analysis
Supportive reasoning affirms the scenario's plausibility in targeted harassment, where bullies exploit weak multi-factor authentication to alter files post-submission, causing stress and grade impacts (Alturif, 2024). This aligns with power imbalances in academia. Counter-arguments highlight rarity: real-world evidence favors accidental corruption or student self-tampering for deadline extensions over sophisticated indirect hacks (Lucas team input, 2026). Edge cases include insider threats by disgruntled peers or malware from shared networks. Nuances involve cross-domain insights, such as blockchain for immutable submissions (emerging best practice). Implications include eroded trust in digital education; practical scalable insights recommend file hashing verification for individuals and organizations. Multiple perspectives reveal victim trauma versus institutional resource constraints. Disinformation risk exists if unverified hacking claims spread without logs, potentially undermining legitimate reports.

### Analysis Limitations

### Federal, State, or Local Laws in Australia
Federal law under the Criminal Code Act 1995 (Cth) prohibits unauthorized access or impairment of data (s 477.1). The Cybercrime Act 2001 addresses electronic interference. In Victoria, the Crimes Act 1958 covers stalking and computer offenses, with penalties up to 10 years for unauthorized impairment of electronic communications. Image-based or menacing online abuse falls under national and state provisions (Youth Law Australia, 2021; Victoria Police, n.d.).

The analyzed scenario underscores vulnerabilities in digital academic workflows, urging proactive measures grounded in evidence and law. By prioritizing verification and support, universities can safeguard integrity while addressing bullying's multifaceted nature.

### Action Steps
1. Preserve all evidence, including local file copies, submission confirmations, screenshots, and timestamps before contacting anyone.
2. Notify your lecturer or course coordinator immediately with documented proof of the original intact file.
3. Contact university IT services to request log reviews and system integrity verification.
4. Secure your account by enabling or updating multi-factor authentication and changing passwords.
5. Scan personal devices for malware using reputable antivirus software.
6. Report the incident formally through the university's student complaints or harassment portal.
7. File a cybercrime report with Victoria Police or via ReportCyber if evidence suggests criminal intent.
8. Consult university counseling services for emotional support and document any academic accommodations needed.
9. Maintain detailed personal records of all communications for potential escalation.
10. Advocate for broader LMS improvements by submitting anonymous feedback to academic governance bodies.

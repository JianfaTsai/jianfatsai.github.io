### Problem Statement
Modern dual-partner households often face competing demands of shared responsibilities, personal recharge needs, workplace scrutiny, and financial volatility, risking relational erosion, opportunity costs, and debt entrapment when routines remain unoptimized (Weinstein et al., 2023).

### Facts
Empirical data indicate that balanced solitude enhances well-being without isolation, while pooled or flexible cash systems correlate with higher relational satisfaction (Garbinsky, 2022). Debt spirals exacerbate distress in low-income cohorts (Szilagyiova, 2015). Workplace perceptions of personal resource use influence evaluations (Moussa, 2015).

### Evidence
Longitudinal studies link autonomous alone time to reduced stress and sustained partnerships (Weinstein et al., 2023). Financial behavior strongly predicts emergency fund adequacy across cultures (Nguyen, 2023). Electronic monitoring reactance affects job attitudes (Brinson, 2024).

Couples’ financial pooling studies reveal harmony gains but justification pressures (Peetz et al., 2023). Debt literature highlights rollover risks (Szilagyiova, 2015; Eriksson, 2025). Workplace EPM research documents privacy calculus effects (Glavin et al., 2024). Frugality aligns with consumer behavior on portion control (Fuster et al., 2021).
